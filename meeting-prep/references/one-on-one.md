# 1:1 Meeting Prep Framework

## What this is for

The 1:1 is the most underutilized meeting in most organizations — and the most important one for career growth. Done well, it's where alignment happens, problems surface early, and the relationship between a manager and direct report actually develops. Done poorly, it's a status update that should have been an email.

This framework covers 1:1s from both sides: as a direct report preparing to meet with your manager, and as a manager preparing to meet with your team.

## PRE mode: as a direct report

**The mindset shift**

Most people treat the 1:1 as the manager's meeting. It's not — it's yours. You own the agenda. Your manager has 7 other direct reports; if you don't bring topics, the meeting fills with whatever is top of mind for them. That's usually status updates, which are the lowest-value use of the time.

**What to bring**

A good 1:1 agenda has 3–4 items, not 10. Prioritize:

**Updates worth discussing** (not just reporting):
- Things that went well and why — share the learning, not just the win
- Things that went wrong or are at risk — better they hear it from you first, with context
- Blockers you need help with — specific, not vague ("I need a decision on X by Friday so we can move forward")

**Topics that need their input or decision:**
- Ambiguous priorities: "I have X and Y on my plate — which matters more to you right now?"
- Resource or access issues: "I need [person / tool / budget] to move this forward — how do we get that?"
- Anything you've been putting off bringing up because it felt awkward — 1:1s are exactly the right venue

**Career and development topics (bring these proactively):**
- What you're working on to grow
- Skills you want to build or projects you want to take on
- Feedback you want on something specific
- Where you see yourself going and whether you're on the right path

If you never bring career topics to your 1:1s, your manager has no visibility into your ambitions. That visibility is necessary for them to advocate for you.

**Feedback to give your manager**

1:1s are the right place for upward feedback — something about how they can support you better, a decision that confused you, or appreciation for something specific they did. Most people skip this. The managers who grow are the ones whose direct reports actually tell them things.

**Running the meeting**

- Share your agenda at the start (even informally): "I have three things I want to cover — can I start with the one that's most time-sensitive?"
- Time-box your updates — no more than 2 minutes each, then move to discussion
- Don't fill the silence with more talking. If they're thinking, let them think.
- End with: "Is there anything you wanted to cover that we didn't get to?"

## PRE mode: as a manager

**What your direct report needs from this meeting**

Before building your agenda, ask what they actually need from you:
- Clarity on priorities and direction?
- Help unblocking something?
- Feedback on their performance?
- A space to surface concerns they haven't been able to raise otherwise?

Your agenda is secondary to theirs.

**Topics to bring as a manager**

- Feedback: specific, timely, actionable — positive and constructive
- Context they don't have: company direction, changes coming, why a decision was made the way it was
- Recognition: specific calls-out for things you noticed — not generic praise
- Career development: where do you see them going, what are they working toward, are you helping them get there?
- Anything you've been meaning to address but haven't — 1:1s prevent issues from accumulating

**Questions that open real conversations:**

- "What's the most frustrating thing about your work right now?"
- "What are you most proud of from the last two weeks?"
- "Is there anything I'm doing — or not doing — that's making your job harder?"
- "What would you change about how we work together?"
- "What do you want to be doing more of?"

**What to avoid as a manager**

- Turning the 1:1 into a status report — that's what Slack is for
- Doing most of the talking — you have the information advantage; use the time to listen
- Skipping or shortening 1:1s when you're busy — that's exactly when your team needs them most

## POST mode: after the 1:1

- Document any commitments made — by you or by them — and follow through
- If action items were generated, confirm them in writing (a quick Slack message or email recap)
- Note anything raised that you want to revisit at the next 1:1
- If you're the manager: update your notes on where this person is, what they need, and what you're watching

## Recurring 1:1 structure (suggested)

For weekly or biweekly 1:1s, a consistent light structure builds good habits without making it feel like a form to fill out:

1. Personal check-in (2 min) — how are they actually doing?
2. Their agenda items (15–20 min)
3. Manager topics (5–10 min)
4. Career / growth topic — rotate through these quarterly so they don't get forgotten

## What to deliver in PRE mode

- 3–4 agenda items, prioritized
- Any specific decisions or inputs needed from the other person
- One career or development topic if appropriate
- Any feedback to give (upward or downward)
