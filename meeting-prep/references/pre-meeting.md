# Pre-Meeting Preparation Framework

## Why pre-meeting prep matters

Most meetings fail before they start. The failure modes are consistent:
- No one is clear on what the meeting is actually for
- People show up with opinions but not questions
- Whoever talks most drives the outcome, not whoever has the best thinking
- The last 5 minutes are chaos because no one owns next steps

Good prep solves all of these. It doesn't mean over-planning — it means knowing what you want to come out of this meeting and how to steer toward it.

## The meeting brief

A meeting brief is a 5-minute read that gives the user everything they need to walk in confident. It covers:

### Why this meeting matters
Not "we're meeting to discuss X" — that's a topic, not a purpose. The brief should answer: *what decision, relationship, or outcome hangs on this meeting going well?*

Examples:
- Weak: "We're meeting to discuss the product roadmap"
- Strong: "This is the moment to align on Q3 priorities before the engineering team locks in their sprint planning. If we leave without a decision on the top 3 features, the next quarter gets decided by whoever speaks up first in Slack."

### What the user wants from each attendee
Different people in the room have different things to offer or decide. Name them:
- "[Name] is the decision-maker on budget — you need their buy-in before anything else moves"
- "[Name] will push back on timeline — come prepared with the dependency map"
- "[Name] is new — worth investing 2 minutes to make them feel included, it pays off later"

### What a successful meeting looks like
One or two sentences, specific:
- "We leave with a signed-off launch date and a single owner for each workstream"
- "They offer a second interview by end of week"
- "We understand what's actually blocking them, even if we don't solve it today"

## The agenda

A meeting without an agenda is a conversation. That's fine for some things — not for most professional meetings.

**Agenda design principles:**
- Each item has a time allocation and an owner (who's driving this segment)
- Each item has a stated goal (decision / discussion / update) — this tells people what mode they're in
- Leave 10–15% of the time for wrap-up and next steps — this almost always gets cut, and when it does, meetings end without clarity
- Order items deliberately: put the most important things early, when energy is high

**Time allocation by meeting length:**

| Meeting length | Agenda items | Buffer |
|---------------|--------------|--------|
| 30 min | 2–3 items | 5 min |
| 60 min | 3–5 items | 10 min |
| 90 min | 5–7 items | 15 min |
| 2 hours | 6–8 items | 20 min |

**Agenda item format:**
```
[Time block] — [Topic] — [Owner] — [Goal: Decision / Discussion / Update]
```

## Questions

This is the most underrated part of meeting prep. Most people prepare *points* — things they want to say. The best meeting participants prepare *questions* — things they want to know.

Questions drive conversations better than statements. They give the other person room to contribute. They surface information you didn't know you needed. They signal intelligence and preparation.

### Three types of questions

**1. Decisions needed**
Questions that require the other party to choose or commit:
- "Are we aligned that X is the priority, or is Y still on the table?"
- "Who is the decision-maker on this if we can't agree?"
- "What would need to be true for you to greenlight this?"

**2. Information needed**
Questions that fill gaps in your understanding:
- "What's driving the timeline pressure on your end?"
- "What's worked (or not worked) when you've tried this before?"
- "What am I not seeing that you think I should know?"

**3. Alignment / relationship**
Questions that build trust, surface concerns, or read the room:
- "What would make this feel like a win for you?"
- "Is there anything about this you're uncertain about that we haven't talked about?"
- "How are you thinking about this relative to [other priority]?"

### How many questions
- 30-minute meeting: 3–5 questions
- 60-minute meeting: 5–8 questions
- For job interviews: 3–5 per interviewer, weighted toward "alignment / relationship" type

### Prioritize them
Mark 1–2 as "must-ask" — the questions without which the meeting hasn't done its job. The rest are good-to-have if time allows.

## Things to watch for

Not every meeting is straightforward. Flag these where relevant:

- **Hidden agenda**: Is someone likely to use this meeting to push a position they haven't disclosed?
- **Power dynamics**: Who actually makes the decision, and is it the same person as who seems to?
- **Unspoken tension**: Any relationship friction, past conflicts, or competing interests that might surface?
- **Landmine topics**: Things likely to derail or create defensiveness if raised at the wrong moment?
- **Timing**: Is there anything about when this meeting is happening that changes the dynamic? (end of quarter, someone is leaving, a decision was just made elsewhere)

## Meeting-type specific notes

### Job interview prep
- Research the company AND the interviewer (LinkedIn, their work, their interests)
- Prepare 2-3 stories using STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) that cover your strongest examples
- Have genuine questions — interviewers notice when questions are generic ("what does a typical day look like?")
- Know what makes you interested in this specific role at this specific company — you'll be asked
- Have a clear answer to: "Tell me about yourself" (2 minutes, narrative not resume recital)

### Client / sales call prep
- Know their business: what do they do, who are their customers, what's their competitive position?
- Know the deal history: what have you discussed before, what did they care about, what objections came up?
- Know your objective for this specific call — demo? Discovery? Close? Don't go in trying to do all three.
- Prepare for "what does this cost?" and "how does this compare to [competitor]?"

### 1:1 with manager
- Bring your own agenda, not just answers to their questions
- Update on your current work: what's going well, what's stuck, what you need from them
- One thing you want their input on (not just FYI — a real question)
- Career topic, if the relationship allows: progress, growth areas, visibility

### Negotiation meeting
- Know your walk-away number before you walk in
- Know their likely constraints — what are they optimizing for?
- Decide in advance what you'll concede easily vs. what you'll hold firm on
- Never give a concession without getting something in return

### Difficult conversation
- Know what outcome you want: apology? behavior change? shared understanding? clarity?
- Separate what happened (facts) from how it made you feel (impact) from what you want (ask)
- Prepare for defensiveness — have a response that doesn't escalate
- Know what your "good enough" outcome is vs. your ideal
