# Job Interview Prep Framework

## What this is for

Job interview prep is distinct from general meeting prep — the stakes are higher, the format is predictable, and the research required is specific. Most interview failures happen before the conversation: candidates don't know the company well enough, haven't prepared their stories, or haven't thought through the questions they'll ask. This framework covers all three.

## Step 1: Research the company and role

**The company**
- What does the company actually do? (Be able to explain it in one sentence)
- What's their business model — how do they make money?
- Recent news: funding, product launches, leadership changes, challenges
- Culture signals: Glassdoor, LinkedIn, their own careers page content
- Who are their main competitors and how do they differentiate?

**The role**
- What problem does this role exist to solve?
- What does success look like in the first 90 days?
- Which parts of the job description are likely day-to-day vs. aspirational?
- What skills or experiences are they weighting most heavily?

**The interviewer(s)**
- LinkedIn profiles of everyone you're meeting
- Their background, tenure, areas of focus
- Any shared connections or overlapping experiences worth noting naturally

## Step 2: Prepare your stories using STAR

Behavioral interview questions ("Tell me about a time when...") are the most common format. Each answer needs a structured story. Use STAR:

- **S**ituation: brief context — where, when, what was the challenge
- **T**ask: your specific responsibility in that situation
- **A**ction: what you specifically did (use "I", not "we")
- **R**esult: what happened, ideally with a measurable outcome

Prepare 5–8 stories that can flex across multiple questions. Common themes to have ready:
- A time you solved a difficult problem
- A time you handled conflict or a difficult colleague
- A time you failed and what you learned
- A time you led something or influenced without authority
- A time you had to work under pressure or meet a tight deadline
- A time you went above and beyond what was asked
- A time you had to adapt to significant change
- Your biggest professional accomplishment

**The "tell me about yourself" answer**
Structure: where you started → key moves that led here → why this role specifically. Keep it to 90 seconds. End on why you're talking to them today — don't make them connect the dots.

## Step 3: Prepare for likely role-specific questions

Beyond behavioral questions, technical or role-specific questions vary by field. Identify 3–5 likely questions based on the job description and prepare direct answers.

Also prepare for:
- "Why are you leaving your current role?" — honest but framed forward, never negative about the current employer
- "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" — show ambition and growth that aligns with the company's trajectory
- "What's your biggest weakness?" — genuine, with evidence of self-awareness and active improvement
- "Why this company specifically?" — this requires real research; generic answers are disqualifying

## Step 4: Prepare questions to ask them

Asking good questions signals preparation and genuine interest. Weak questions ("What's the culture like?") are forgettable. Strong questions show you've thought about the role and the business.

Examples by category:

**About the role:**
- "What does success look like in the first 6 months — what would tell you this hire worked out?"
- "What are the biggest challenges the person in this role will face?"
- "What do the strongest people on this team have in common?"

**About the team:**
- "How does this team make decisions?"
- "What's the biggest thing the team is focused on right now?"

**About the company:**
- "Where do you see the company in 2–3 years?"
- "What's changed most about the company since you joined?"

**About the interviewer:**
- "What do you like most about working here?" (genuine, surfaces real signals)

Avoid: questions about salary and benefits (too early unless they bring it up), questions easily answered by the website, and more than 3–4 questions total.

## Step 5: Logistics and mindset

- Confirm time, location/link, format, and who you're meeting — the day before
- If virtual: test camera, microphone, background, and internet 30 minutes before
- Arrive (or log in) 5–10 minutes early; not more
- Bring a printed copy of your resume if in-person — have the file open if virtual
- Take brief notes during the interview — it's professional and helps with follow-up

**After the interview:**
Send a thank-you email within 24 hours — brief, specific to something discussed in the conversation, not a generic template. (See email-writer for the thank-you framework.)

## What to deliver in PRE mode

- Company and role research summary
- "Tell me about yourself" draft (90-second version)
- 5–8 STAR stories mapped to likely question themes
- 4–5 prepared questions to ask
- Logistics checklist
