# Post-Meeting Follow-up Framework

## Why post-meeting follow-up matters

The most common failure mode after a good meeting is that nothing happens. The decisions made in the room get fuzzy within 48 hours. Action items exist in three people's heads in three different forms. Follow-up emails are either never sent or so vague they don't create accountability.

A strong post-meeting output converts the energy of the meeting into durable commitments and clear next steps.

## Working with messy inputs

Post-meeting notes are almost never clean. Expect to receive:
- Bullet points with no context
- Shorthand and abbreviations only the user understands
- Missing names or incomplete references ("talked about the thing with finance")
- Out-of-order notes with backtracking
- Voice memo transcriptions full of filler words and false starts

This is normal. Work with what you have:
- Ask for clarification only on things that are load-bearing (who owns an action item, what the decision actually was)
- Infer what you can from context
- Where something is ambiguous, flag it in brackets: [Clarify: did they agree to X or just discuss it?]
- Don't ask the user to re-explain the whole meeting — extract what you can and surface the specific gaps

## The meeting summary

The summary is a structured record of what happened. It should be accurate, readable, and short enough that people actually read it.

### What goes in a summary

**Decisions made**
The most important section. These are commitments, choices, and conclusions the group reached. Not "we discussed" — "we decided." Be precise:
- Weak: "We talked about the launch date"
- Strong: "Agreed to target October 15th launch; [Name] to confirm with engineering by Thursday"

**Key discussion points**
A concise narrative of what was covered — not a transcript, not a list of every topic, but enough context that someone who wasn't there understands what happened. 3–5 sentences is usually right.

**Open questions / parking lot**
Things that came up but weren't resolved. These are important — if you don't capture them, they get raised again at the next meeting.

### What does NOT go in a summary
- Word-for-word dialogue
- Who said what (unless attribution matters for accountability)
- Everything that was discussed — only what resulted in a decision or action
- Your opinions on how the meeting went

### Summary length
- 30-minute meeting: 150–200 words
- 60-minute meeting: 200–350 words
- 90+ minute meeting: 300–500 words

If it's longer, you're capturing too much.

## Action items

Action items are the most important output of a post-meeting follow-up. They are also the most commonly done wrong.

### The three required fields
Every action item must have:
1. **What**: A specific, concrete task — not "follow up on X" but "send the revised proposal to Sarah"
2. **Who**: A named person, not "the team"
3. **When**: A date or explicit "TBD" — "ASAP" is not a deadline

### Common action item mistakes
- **No owner**: "Someone will look into this" — this means no one will look into it
- **Too vague**: "Follow up on the budget" — follow up how? With whom? About what specifically?
- **No deadline**: Without a date, the urgency disappears immediately after the meeting
- **Too many**: If a 60-minute meeting produces 15 action items, they won't all happen. Prioritize.

### Action item table format
```
| Action | Owner | Due |
|--------|-------|-----|
| [Specific task] | [Name] | [Date] |
```

### For the user's own action items
List these separately or highlight them — the user needs to be clear on what *they* specifically are committed to.

## Follow-up emails

### Who gets what

Different audiences need different things:

**All-attendees summary email**
- Short: 150–200 words max
- Decisions made, action items, next meeting if applicable
- Tone: factual, collaborative
- Timing: within 24 hours of the meeting (same day is better)

**Specific action holder**
- Only needed when an action item belongs to someone who needs a nudge or clear context
- Short: 3–5 sentences
- Tone: direct, collegial
- Content: what they own, by when, and any context they need

**Your manager / stakeholder (keep-in-the-loop email)**
- Summary of outcome, what you learned, what you need from them (if anything)
- Decision-focused: what was resolved, what's still open
- Tone: professional, concise

**The other party (client, interviewer, external contact)**
- This is where tone matters most — refer to the email-writer skill for specifics
- Usually combines: thanks for their time + summary of what was agreed + clear next step

### What makes a good follow-up email

The follow-up email is not just a record — it's a relationship tool. A well-sent follow-up:
- Demonstrates that you were paying attention
- Reinforces agreements before anyone can misremember them
- Creates accountability in writing
- Shows professionalism and follow-through

Keep it short. Organize it clearly. Don't make people read through paragraphs to find the action items.

### Email template for all-attendees

```
Subject: [Meeting name] — Summary & next steps [Date]

Hi [Name / team],

Thanks for the time today. Quick summary:

**Decisions made:**
- [Decision 1]
- [Decision 2]

**Action items:**
- [Name]: [Action] by [date]
- [Name]: [Action] by [date]

Next meeting: [date/time, or "TBD — [Name] to schedule"]

Let me know if I've missed anything.

[Your name]
```

## Post-meeting reflection (optional, for the user's benefit)

For important meetings (job interviews, significant client calls, difficult conversations), include a brief reflection section for the user's own use — not to be sent:

- What went well?
- What would you do differently?
- What did you learn about the other person / the situation?
- What's your read on the outcome? (honest assessment)
- What's your next move?

This is especially useful for job interviews and negotiations, where understanding what you learned about *them* is as important as what they learned about you.

## Timing guidance

| Meeting type | When to send follow-up |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Job interview | Same day, within a few hours |
| Client / sales call | Same day or next morning |
| Internal team meeting | Within 24 hours |
| 1:1 with manager | Same day if action items, otherwise not required |
| Networking / informational | Within 24–48 hours |
| Difficult conversation | Allow 24 hours for things to settle; then follow up if commitments were made |
