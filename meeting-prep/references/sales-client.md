# Sales / Client Meeting Prep Framework

## What this is for

Sales and client meetings fail most often because the person running them knows too much about their own product and too little about the person across the table. Good prep shifts the focus from "how do I present what we do" to "what does this person need, and is there a fit?" This applies to formal sales meetings, client check-ins, new business pitches, and account renewals.

## PRE mode: preparing for the meeting

**Step 1: Research the person and the organization**

About the company:
- What do they do and how do they make money?
- Recent news: growth, challenges, leadership changes, new products
- Size, stage, and likely budget constraints
- Who are their customers — and what pressures do those customers put on them?

About the person you're meeting:
- Their role and tenure
- Their LinkedIn — background, what they've worked on, what they post about
- Are they a decision-maker, influencer, or gatekeeper? (This changes your approach entirely)
- Have they worked with similar vendors or solutions before?

**Step 2: Define what success looks like for this meeting**

Not every sales meeting is a close. Know exactly what outcome moves things forward:
- First meeting: qualify the opportunity, understand their situation, book a second meeting
- Demo / presentation: generate genuine interest, identify the decision process
- Proposal meeting: agree on the problem, align on value, understand remaining objections
- Close: get a decision — yes, no, or a clear next step

Don't try to close in a meeting that should qualify. Don't educate in a meeting that should close.

**Step 3: Prepare discovery questions**

Discovery is the most underrated part of any sales or client conversation. The goal is to understand their situation deeply before you talk about your solution. Prepare questions across:

**Situation questions** (understand the current state):
- "Walk me through how you currently handle [problem area] — what does that look like day to day?"
- "How long has this been the process?"
- "Who else is involved in this?"

**Problem questions** (surface the pain):
- "Where does the current approach break down?"
- "What's the cost of that — in time, money, or missed opportunity?"
- "How often does that happen?"

**Implication questions** (deepen the pain):
- "What happens downstream when [problem] occurs?"
- "Has this affected [other team / metric / relationship]?"

**Need-payoff questions** (connect to value):
- "If you could fix that, what would change?"
- "What would it mean for your team if you had [outcome]?"

Don't fire all of these in sequence — it feels like an interrogation. Weave them into a conversation.

**Step 4: Anticipate objections**

Prepare a response to the 3–4 most likely objections for this specific prospect:

| Objection | What it usually means | How to respond |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| "We don't have budget" | Not a priority yet, or wrong person | Understand their cycle; quantify the cost of inaction |
| "We're happy with our current solution" | Low pain or haven't seen a better option | Ask what "happy" looks like; explore where the gaps are |
| "We need to think about it" | Missing information or undisclosed concern | "What would help you make a decision?" |
| "The timing isn't right" | Real timing issue or a soft no | Agree on when right timing would be; stay in contact |

Objections are information, not rejection. Prepare to explore them, not overcome them.

**Step 5: Know your walk-away**

Before any negotiation or close, define:
- Your ideal outcome (price, scope, timeline)
- Your acceptable outcome (where you'll still say yes)
- Your walk-away (what you won't agree to, and why)

Knowing your walk-away in advance prevents pressure-driven decisions in the room.

## POST mode: after the meeting

- Send a follow-up email within 24 hours: what was discussed, what was agreed, next steps with owners and dates (see email-writer for follow-up framework)
- Update your CRM or notes with: what they told you about their situation, who the decision-makers are, what their timeline looks like, what objections came up
- Did the meeting change your read on the opportunity? Flag if the deal is weaker or stronger than you thought going in

## Client check-in meetings (existing accounts)

For ongoing client relationships, prep looks different:
- Review their usage, activity, or engagement data before the call
- Know their renewal date and contract terms
- Identify any recent issues (support tickets, complaints) and be ready to address them proactively — don't wait for them to bring it up
- Have at least one piece of value to deliver (an insight, a tip, a relevant case study)
- Know what expansion or upsell might be relevant — and whether this is the right meeting for it

The goal of a check-in is to reinforce that the relationship is worth maintaining. Show up prepared, not just present.

## What to deliver in PRE mode

- Company and contact research summary
- Meeting objective (what success looks like for this specific meeting)
- 6–8 discovery questions tailored to their situation
- Anticipated objections with prepared responses
- Proposed next step to close the meeting with
