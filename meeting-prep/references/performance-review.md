# Performance Review Prep Framework

## What this is for

Performance reviews are one of the highest-leverage conversations a professional has — they affect compensation, trajectory, and how you're perceived by leadership. Most people underprepare. They walk in hoping their work speaks for itself, when the reality is that the person across the table has 8 other direct reports and a limited view of what you actually did. Your job is to make your contributions visible, specific, and memorable — before, during, and after the review.

This framework covers two directions: preparing to present your own performance, and preparing to receive feedback.

## PRE mode: preparing for your own review

**Step 1: Build your evidence file**

Pull everything from the review period:
- Projects completed, contributions made — with specific outcomes where possible
- Metrics you own or influenced (revenue, growth, efficiency, quality, speed)
- Problems you solved that weren't part of your original scope
- People you helped, trained, or mentored
- Feedback you received (positive and constructive) and how you acted on it
- Goals set at the last review — which did you hit, miss, or exceed?

Be specific. "Improved the process" is weak. "Cut the onboarding time from 3 weeks to 10 days by rebuilding the training materials" is a contribution.

**Step 2: Frame your accomplishments in impact language**

Translate activities into outcomes. The structure: **what you did → what it produced → why it mattered.**

- Weak: "Managed the Q3 campaign"
- Strong: "Led the Q3 campaign end-to-end — it generated 2,400 leads against a target of 1,800, and three of those accounts are now in late-stage pipeline"

If you don't have hard numbers, use relative terms: "reduced", "improved", "eliminated", "faster than", "first time the team had ever".

**Step 3: Address the gaps proactively**

If you missed a goal or had a rough patch, don't hope they don't bring it up — they will. Prepare a clear-eyed account:
- What happened
- What you learned
- What you've changed since

Owning a failure confidently is more impressive than getting defensive about it.

**Step 4: Know what you want from this conversation**

Performance reviews have outcomes beyond the rating. Know what you're there to accomplish:
- A compensation adjustment? Know the number you want and why it's justified.
- A promotion conversation? Know what criteria you've met and which you're still working toward.
- Clearer direction? Prepare specific questions about priorities and growth.
- Recognition of specific contributions? Know which ones you want acknowledged.

**Step 5: Prepare for the compensation conversation**

If salary is on the table:
- Know your market rate (use salary data from your industry and region)
- Have a specific number, not a range — ranges anchor to the low end
- Frame it around contribution and market, not personal need
- If you can't get the number you want, know what else you'd take: a title change, extra PTO, a 6-month review, a development budget

## POST mode: after the review

**If it went well:**
- Document what was said — ratings, specific feedback, commitments made by your manager
- Set a reminder to follow up on anything promised (comp changes, promotions, development plans)
- Note what you should keep doing

**If it was critical or disappointing:**
- Don't react in the room — listen, take notes, thank them for the feedback
- Give yourself 24 hours before responding or deciding anything
- Ask one follow-up question before leaving: "What would meaningful improvement look like over the next quarter?"
- Request a follow-up meeting to discuss a plan if the feedback was significant

**If there was a disagreement:**
- You can acknowledge feedback without agreeing with it: "I hear that — I'd like to share my perspective as well"
- Keep a record if you believe the evaluation is unfair; HR processes require documentation

## What to deliver in PRE mode

- Accomplishment summary (bulleted, impact-framed, ready to reference or share)
- Prepared responses to likely criticism
- Salary / promotion talking points if relevant
- 2–3 questions to ask your manager about your trajectory and next steps
