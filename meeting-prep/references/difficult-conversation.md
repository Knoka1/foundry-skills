# Difficult Conversation Prep Framework

## What this is for

Difficult conversations are the ones people rehearse in the shower, put off for weeks, and then either deliver too harshly (pent-up frustration) or too softly (conflict avoidance) when they finally happen. This framework covers the prep work that makes the actual conversation more likely to go well: clarifying what you want to say, anticipating how it will land, and structuring the conversation so both people leave with clarity rather than wounds.

Types of difficult conversations this applies to:
- Giving critical feedback to a colleague or direct report
- Addressing conflict between two people
- Setting a boundary with a manager, peer, or client
- Delivering bad news (a missed deadline, a budget cut, a role change)
- Ending a professional relationship (vendor, contractor, colleague)
- Raising a complaint or concern with someone who has more power than you

## Step 1: Get clear on what you actually want to say

Most difficult conversations go badly because the person having them hasn't separated three things:

**The facts**: What actually happened, observable and specific. Not "you're always late" but "the last three deliverables were submitted after the agreed deadline."

**The impact**: What that created. Not "it was frustrating" but "it caused us to miss the client review, and I had to reschedule a call."

**The request**: What you want to change. Not vague ("I need you to be more reliable") but specific ("I need deliverables by noon on the agreed date, or I need 24 hours notice if that's not going to happen").

Write these three things out before the meeting. If you can't state the request specifically, you're not ready to have the conversation.

## Step 2: Examine your own role

Almost every workplace conflict has contributions from both sides. Before the conversation, honestly ask:
- Have I been clear about expectations?
- Did I create any of the conditions that led to this?
- Is there something in my own behavior that makes this person's situation harder?

This isn't about self-blame — it's about going into the conversation without blind spots. If you've contributed to the problem, own it. It disarms defensiveness and signals that you're in problem-solving mode, not prosecution mode.

## Step 3: Anticipate how they'll receive it

Put yourself in the other person's position:
- How are they likely to react? Defensive? Upset? Surprised?
- What's their version of events likely to be?
- Is there context you don't have that might change the picture?
- What are they afraid of? (Job security? Being judged? The relationship changing?)

Anticipating their reaction isn't about softening the message — it's about being prepared to hear things that complicate your view, and not being thrown off when they do.

## Step 4: Plan the opening

The first 30 seconds of a difficult conversation set the entire tone. A bad opening creates defensiveness that you'll spend the rest of the conversation managing.

**What works:**
- State the purpose directly and without ambiguity: "I want to talk about [specific thing] because it's affecting [specific outcome] and I want us to fix it."
- Signal collaboration, not attack: "I want to work through this together — I have a perspective, and I want to hear yours."
- Be private and calm — difficult conversations don't belong in public, in writing if avoidable, or in moments of high emotion

**What doesn't work:**
- The "compliment sandwich" — people see through it and it dilutes the message
- Starting with "I just wanted to..." — undercuts your own credibility
- Apologizing for having the conversation — you're there for a reason, own it
- Ambushing — if the topic is serious, give advance notice: "I'd like to talk about X — can we find 30 minutes this week?"

## Step 5: Structure the conversation

A framework for the conversation itself:

1. **Open**: State the purpose clearly (see Step 4)
2. **Share your perspective**: Facts → impact → what you need. Specific, calm, no editorializing
3. **Invite their perspective**: "I want to understand your view on this." Then actually listen — don't use this as a pause before saying more
4. **Find the gap**: Where do your views align? Where do they differ? The conversation lives in the gap.
5. **Agree on next steps**: Specific, named, with a timeline. Both people should leave knowing exactly what happens next.

## Handling what comes up in the room

**If they get defensive**: Don't escalate. Pause. Acknowledge: "I hear that this landed differently than I intended." Return to the facts.

**If they get emotional**: Give them space. Silence is okay. Don't rush past the emotion to get back to the agenda.

**If they reveal something you didn't know**: Adjust. The new information matters. "I didn't know that — it changes my understanding of the situation. Can you tell me more?"

**If they deflect or make it about something else**: "I want to hear that too — can we talk about [original topic] first and then come back to that?"

**If they shut down completely**: Don't force resolution. "It seems like this is a lot to take in. Can we pick this back up tomorrow?"

## POST mode: after the conversation

- Follow up in writing if commitments were made — a brief email recap: "Following up on our conversation — we agreed to X and Y, with Z as the timeline."
- Give it time to land before assessing whether it worked
- If nothing changed: one more direct conversation before escalating
- If it escalated or involves serious misconduct: document everything and involve HR or leadership

## What to deliver in PRE mode

- The three-part statement: facts, impact, request — written out
- Your honest assessment of your own role in the situation
- Anticipated reactions and how you'll respond to each
- A planned opening sentence
- Proposed next steps you'll suggest at the end
