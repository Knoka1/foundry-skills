# Goal Tracking Framework

## What this is for

Goal tracking connects someone's day-to-day financial behavior to the specific outcomes they're working toward. Most financial analysis is backward-looking — what did I spend last month? Goal tracking is forward-looking: given what I earn and spend today, am I on track for what I actually want? A budget without a goal is just accounting. A goal without a plan is just a wish.

The core principle: **turn a vague financial goal into a specific monthly number, then check whether current behavior supports it.**

## Step 1: Define the goal clearly

A useful financial goal has four components:

| Component | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **What**: the specific outcome | Buy a car |
| **How much**: the target amount | $8,000 (down payment + fees) |
| **When**: the target date | 18 months from now |
| **Monthly savings required**: the math | $8,000 ÷ 18 = ~$445/month |

Push for specificity on all four. "I want to save more" is not a goal. "I want $15,000 for a home down payment by December 2026" is a goal.

Common goal categories to prompt for:
- **Emergency fund**: 3–6 months of essential expenses
- **Home purchase**: down payment + closing costs (typically 3–20% of home price + ~2–5% for closing)
- **Car purchase**: cash purchase or down payment target
- **Vacation or travel**: total trip cost
- **Education**: tuition, certifications, courses
- **Wedding**: total estimated cost
- **Debt freedom**: specific debt or all consumer debt paid off
- **Early retirement / FIRE**: nest egg target (25x annual expenses rule of thumb)
- **Business or side project**: startup costs

## Step 2: Calculate the monthly savings target

Monthly savings needed = (Goal amount - Current savings toward goal) ÷ Months remaining

If they already have some savings toward the goal, subtract it. The number tells them exactly what they need to set aside each month.

## Step 3: Check feasibility against current finances

Compare the monthly savings target to:
- Current savings rate: are they saving enough overall?
- Current discretionary spending: where could the monthly target come from?
- Fixed obligations: is there structural room?

Three outcomes:
1. **On track**: Current savings behavior already covers the goal. Surface this — it's validating and rare.
2. **Reachable with adjustment**: The gap is closeable with specific spending changes or modest income additions. Show exactly what changes would close it.
3. **Timeline or amount needs to change**: The goal as defined isn't feasible with current income and obligations. Present the trade-offs — extend the timeline, reduce the target, or increase income — without recommending which.

## Step 4: Handle multiple goals

Most people have more than one goal at a time. When goals compete for the same dollars:

1. **Rank by urgency and importance**: Emergency fund is almost always first — without it, any financial shock derails every other goal.
2. **Identify sequential vs. parallel goals**: Some goals can be funded simultaneously; others are better funded sequentially (finish one before starting another).
3. **Show the full picture**: List all goals with their monthly requirements and total them. If the total exceeds what's available, the person needs to prioritize or adjust — show them the math, let them choose.

Priority guidance (neutral, not prescriptive):
- Emergency fund (if below 1 month of expenses): urgent
- High-interest debt payoff: typically before long-term savings goals
- Employer retirement match: capture before other goals (free money)
- Short-term goals (under 12 months): keep in liquid savings, not invested
- Long-term goals (3+ years): may benefit from investing, though don't recommend specific products

## Step 5: Build a progress tracker

For ongoing goal tracking, structure a simple monthly check-in:

| Goal | Target | Target date | Monthly needed | Saved so far | On track? |
|------|--------|------------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| Emergency fund | $9,000 | Dec 2025 | $750 | $2,400 | Behind |
| Car down payment | $5,000 | Jun 2026 | $280 | $840 | On track |

Surface whether they're ahead, on track, or behind — and by how much.

## What to deliver

- Each goal clearly defined with target amount, date, and monthly savings required
- Feasibility assessment: on track / reachable with adjustment / needs timeline or amount change
- If multiple goals: a priority stack and a monthly savings total required across all goals
- Specific gap analysis: where the monthly target would come from in their current budget
- A simple progress table they can use to check in over time

Do not recommend specific savings accounts, investment vehicles, or financial products. Do not project specific investment returns. For long-term goals involving investing, note that a financial advisor can help with product selection.
