# Savings Rate Analysis Framework

## What this is for

Savings rate analysis tells someone how much of their income they're actually keeping — and whether that rate is aligned with their financial situation and goals. It's a single number that cuts through the complexity of budgeting and reveals the fundamental health of someone's finances. A high income with a low savings rate is fragile; a modest income with a high savings rate is resilient.

The core principle: **savings rate is the most important number in personal finance. Everything else is detail.**

## Step 1: Calculate savings rate

**Savings rate = (Total monthly savings) ÷ (Gross monthly income) × 100**

Some use net income as the denominator — either is valid, just be consistent. Gross income is more commonly used for benchmarking.

**What counts as savings:**
- Contributions to retirement accounts (401k, IRA, pension)
- Employer match contributions (it's money being saved on their behalf)
- Contributions to HSA or FSA
- Transfers to savings accounts, emergency fund, or investment accounts
- Extra debt payments beyond minimums (this is effectively forced savings)

**What does not count as savings:**
- Minimum debt payments (these are obligations, not savings)
- Money spent on one-time large purchases (even if they felt disciplined)

## Step 2: Benchmark against context

Savings rate benchmarks vary significantly by life stage and goal:

| Situation | Typical savings rate target |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Early career, just starting out | 5–15% |
| Mid-career, building wealth | 15–25% |
| Aggressive savings / early retirement goal | 30–50%+ |
| High income, lower obligations | 25–40% |
| Paying off high-interest debt aggressively | Debt payoff counts — 15–25% total |
| Near retirement, catching up | 20–30%+ |

The commonly cited benchmark is **15–20% of gross income** for a stable retirement timeline. Below 10% is a warning sign unless there's a specific short-term reason (high debt payoff, early career, etc.).

**Average US household savings rate** (for context, not as a target): typically 3–8%. Being average here is not a goal — average retirement readiness in the US is poor.

## Step 3: Project the impact

Show what their current savings rate means in practice. Key projections:

**Emergency fund timeline**: At their current savings rate, how long to reach a 3-month or 6-month emergency fund (if not already there)?

**Retirement readiness (simplified)**: Using the 25x rule — a rough estimate of the nest egg needed to retire is 25 times annual expenses. At their current savings rate and assuming a modest real return (3–5%), how many years to reach that target? Present as a range, not a precise forecast, and note this is illustrative.

**Goal timelines**: For any specific savings goals they've mentioned, calculate how long at their current rate vs. an improved rate.

Do not provide specific investment return projections or financial planning advice. Use ranges and clearly label estimates as illustrative.

## Step 4: Surface the levers

If savings rate is below their target, identify the two levers:

**Increase income**: Side income, negotiating a raise, taking on additional work. Note that income increases often don't improve savings rate unless spending is held constant.

**Decrease spending**: Which categories have the most room? Reference the spending data if available. Fixed costs are harder to cut but have larger impact; discretionary is easier but smaller impact per category.

Present both levers neutrally — the person decides which to pursue.

## Step 5: Identify the auto-save opportunity

The most reliable way to improve savings rate is to make it automatic and invisible — savings that never hit the checking account don't get spent.

Surface these options for them to research with their employer or bank:
- Increasing 401k contribution percentage
- Setting up automatic transfer to savings on payday
- Directing a portion of any raise directly to savings before adjusting lifestyle

Note: lifestyle inflation (spending rising in proportion to income increases) is the most common reason savings rates don't improve despite income growth.

## What to deliver

- Current savings rate (calculated, with breakdown of what was counted)
- Benchmark comparison for their life stage
- Simple projection: what their savings rate means for emergency fund, retirement timeline, or specific goals
- Two or three observations about what's working and where the biggest opportunity is
- The auto-save framing if relevant

Do not recommend specific savings accounts, investment vehicles, or financial advisors. Do not project specific investment returns.
