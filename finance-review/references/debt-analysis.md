# Debt Analysis Framework

## What this is for

Debt analysis helps someone understand what they owe, what it's costing them, and in what order to pay it down. The goal is clarity — total picture, real cost, actionable sequence — without judgment and without recommending specific financial products.

The core principle: **understand the full cost of debt, then prioritize by math or psychology depending on what the person needs.**

## Step 1: Build the debt inventory

Collect all debts into one view. For each:

| Field | What to capture |
|-------|----------------|
| Debt name | Credit card, student loan, car loan, personal loan, medical, etc. |
| Current balance | Outstanding principal |
| Interest rate (APR) | Annual percentage rate |
| Minimum payment | Required monthly minimum |
| Monthly payment (actual) | What they're currently paying |
| Remaining term | Months left (if fixed-term loan) |

If the person doesn't know all of these, note the gap — unknown interest rates are a common blind spot and often the source of the problem.

## Step 2: Calculate the real cost

For each debt, compute:
- **Monthly interest cost** = Balance × (APR / 12)
- **Percentage of payment going to interest** = Monthly interest ÷ Monthly payment

Surface the ones where the majority of the payment is going to interest, not principal. This is usually the most clarifying moment in a debt review — people often don't realize they're barely moving the balance.

**Total monthly debt cost** = Sum of all minimum payments. Flag if this exceeds 20% of take-home income (standard debt-to-income warning threshold) or 36% (high-risk threshold).

## Step 3: Identify high-interest debt

Separate debts into two categories:

**High-interest debt** (typically 8%+ APR): Credit cards, payday loans, personal loans, some car loans. These should be the priority target.

**Lower-interest debt** (typically under 6–7% APR): Federal student loans, mortgages, some car loans. These are often worth carrying — the math of paying these off aggressively vs. saving or investing is less clear-cut.

Do not recommend which to do — just present the distinction and the numbers.

## Step 4: Present the two payoff strategies

**Avalanche method (mathematically optimal)**
Pay minimums on all debts. Direct every extra dollar to the highest-interest debt first. Once it's paid off, roll that payment to the next highest. Minimizes total interest paid.

Limitation: If the highest-interest debt also has the largest balance, progress is slow at first. Some people lose motivation.

**Snowball method (psychologically effective)**
Pay minimums on all debts. Direct extra dollars to the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. Quick wins maintain momentum.

Limitation: Costs more in interest over time, sometimes significantly.

Present both with rough estimates of total interest paid and payoff timeline under each strategy, using whatever monthly extra payment the person can afford. Let them choose.

## Step 5: Calculate payoff timeline

For the chosen strategy, provide:
- Month-by-month debt payoff sequence (which debt clears first, second, etc.)
- Estimated date each debt is paid off
- Total interest paid under this approach
- What the monthly cash flow looks like once each debt clears (the "debt-free dividend")

The debt-free dividend is motivating — showing someone they'll have $400/month free in 14 months is more concrete than abstract interest savings.

## Flags to surface

- **Credit card with minimum-only payments**: At typical rates (20–28% APR) a $5,000 balance on minimum payments can take 15+ years to pay off and cost more in interest than the original balance.
- **High debt-to-income ratio**: Total minimums above 20% of take-home income creates significant financial fragility.
- **Unknown interest rates**: Flag these — they can't make good decisions without knowing the cost.
- **Debt consolidation context**: If multiple high-interest debts exist, a consolidation loan at a lower rate is a legitimate tool to mention as something to research with their bank. Do not recommend specific products.

## What to deliver

- Debt inventory table with real costs calculated
- Total monthly debt burden as % of take-home income
- Avalanche vs. snowball comparison (timeline + total interest)
- Recommended starting point based on their situation (largest balance vs. highest rate)
- Payoff milestone dates

Do not recommend specific lenders, credit products, or refinancing options beyond noting they exist as tools to research.
