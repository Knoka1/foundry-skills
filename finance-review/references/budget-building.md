# Budget Building Framework

## What this is for

Budget building is proactive — it's for someone who wants to plan where their money should go, not just understand where it went. It starts with income, works through fixed obligations, and carves out space for goals before addressing discretionary spending. The order matters: most people budget leftover-first and wonder why they never save.

The core principle: **pay yourself first, then build the rest of the budget around what's left.**

## Step 1: Establish take-home income

Start with net income (after tax, after deductions). If income is variable (freelance, hourly, commission), use a conservative baseline — the floor of a typical month, not the average.

If the person has multiple income sources, list each separately:
- Primary employment (net)
- Side income (net estimate)
- Passive income (dividends, rental, etc.)

**Total monthly take-home = the budget ceiling.** Everything must fit within this number.

## Step 2: Identify fixed obligations

These are non-negotiable monthly outflows — amounts that don't change and can't easily be skipped:

- Rent / mortgage
- Loan payments (car, student, personal)
- Insurance premiums (health, auto, renters/homeowners)
- Subscriptions with annual commitments
- Minimum debt payments (if carrying balances)
- Childcare, alimony, or other fixed obligations

Calculate the total. This is immovable. If fixed obligations exceed 60–65% of take-home income, flag it — the budget has structural pressure that discretionary cuts alone won't fix.

## Step 3: Carve out savings and goals before spending

This is where most budgets fail — savings get treated as whatever's left over. Left-over savings are usually zero.

Identify their savings goals and assign a monthly amount to each:
- **Emergency fund** (if not already 3–6 months of expenses): prioritize this first
- **Short-term goals**: vacation, car, home down payment — specific target + timeline = monthly amount needed
- **Retirement contributions**: if employer-matched, contribute enough to capture the full match — that's an immediate 50–100% return

Move these amounts off the table before allocating discretionary spending.

## Step 4: Allocate discretionary spending

What's left after fixed obligations and savings is the discretionary budget. Divide it across:

- Groceries and household
- Dining out / takeaway
- Transportation (fuel, transit, parking)
- Entertainment and subscriptions
- Clothing
- Personal care
- Miscellaneous / buffer

A rough benchmark for discretionary as a share of take-home:

| Financial situation | Discretionary target |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Paying off high-interest debt | 15–25% |
| Building emergency fund | 25–35% |
| Stable, goals-focused | 30–40% |
| High income, low obligations | 40–50% |

## Benchmark frameworks

Two common frameworks to reference or adapt:

**50/30/20**
- 50% needs (fixed obligations + essentials)
- 30% wants (discretionary)
- 20% savings and debt repayment beyond minimums

Works well as a starting point. Flag when someone's needs are above 60% — the 50/30/20 math breaks down.

**Zero-based budgeting**
Every dollar is assigned a purpose. Income minus all allocations = 0. More precise, more effort. Good for people serious about aggressive savings goals or debt payoff.

## Common situations

**Income is irregular**: Build the budget on the lowest-earning month of the past 6. Any income above that goes to a buffer fund first, then goals, then discretionary.

**Expenses exceed income**: Don't paper over this with optimism. Identify which fixed obligations can be renegotiated or eliminated, and which discretionary categories to cut. Be specific — "spend less on dining" is not a plan; "$200/month on dining" is.

**No savings at all**: Emergency fund before everything else. Even $25/month builds the habit. The goal is the behavior, not the amount.

## What to deliver

- A month-by-month budget table: income, fixed obligations, savings allocations, discretionary by category
- A summary of how the budget compares to benchmarks
- One or two observations about where there's structural risk or opportunity — neutral, specific, not prescriptive
- If the numbers don't add up, say so clearly and show what would need to change

Do not project investment returns, recommend specific financial products, or provide tax advice.
