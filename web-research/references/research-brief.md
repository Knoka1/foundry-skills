# Research Brief Framework

## What a research brief is

A research brief is a structured document that captures everything someone needs to know about a topic to make a decision, prepare for a conversation, or act on the information. It's not a Wikipedia summary — it's a curated, opinionated synthesis written for a specific person and purpose.

The test of a good brief: the reader should be able to stop reading at any point and still have something useful. Lead with the most important information.

## Structure

### 1. One-line summary
The single most important thing to know. If the user reads nothing else, this is what they should take away.

*"Remote work arrangements improve individual productivity but reduce spontaneous collaboration — the trade-off depends heavily on role type and team structure."*

### 2. Background (2–4 sentences)
What someone needs to know to understand the findings. Context, not history. Skip anything the user already knows.

### 3. Key findings
The 3–6 most important things the research uncovered, organized by importance — not chronology, not source order.

For each finding:
- State it clearly and specifically
- Note the confidence level (well-established / supported by some evidence / contested)
- Cite the source inline

Format: prose for narrative topics, bullets for factual findings that don't connect into a story.

### 4. Competing perspectives (if relevant)
For any topic where reasonable people disagree, present the strongest version of each major view. This is not a "on the other hand" section — it's a genuine representation of what each side argues and why.

Don't editorialize. If the evidence favors one view, note that — but don't selectively omit the other side's best arguments.

### 5. Open questions
What the research didn't answer. What's still contested. What would change the picture if you knew it. This is often the most valuable section — it tells the user where to dig next and what to be cautious about.

### 6. Sources
List every source used, with enough information to find it: author, publication, date, and URL or title. Group by quality tier if the sources vary significantly in reliability.

## Length calibration

| Research depth | Target length |
|---------------|--------------|
| Quick orientation | 200–400 words |
| Working brief | 400–800 words |
| Thorough reference | 800–1,500 words |
| Deep dive | 1,500+ words (only when explicitly requested) |

Match the length to what the user asked for. A quick question doesn't need a 1,000-word brief. A high-stakes decision might.

## What makes a brief bad

- **Burying the finding**: The most important thing appears in paragraph four. Lead with it.
- **Equal treatment of unequal sources**: A peer-reviewed study and a Reddit post don't deserve the same weight. Signal the difference.
- **False balance**: Presenting fringe views as equally valid to mainstream consensus is misleading. Note when one view is significantly more supported than another.
- **Padding**: More words is not more thorough. Cut anything that doesn't add to the user's understanding.
- **Hedging on everything**: Some things are well-established. Say so clearly instead of qualifying every sentence.
