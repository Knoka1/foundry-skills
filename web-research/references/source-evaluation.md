# Source Evaluation Framework

## Why source evaluation matters

Any research output is only as reliable as its sources. Including a weak source without flagging it misleads the user. The goal isn't to only use perfect sources — those often don't exist — but to accurately represent the quality and reliability of what you found.

## The source quality tiers

### Tier 1 — Primary and authoritative
Highest reliability. Use these as the foundation.

- Peer-reviewed academic research (published in journals with editorial review)
- Official government data (census, health agencies, statistical bureaus)
- Original studies and raw datasets published by research institutions
- First-person accounts from named, credentialed sources speaking about their direct experience
- Legally filed documents, court records, official reports

**How to cite:** author, institution, publication/journal, year, DOI or URL.

### Tier 2 — Credible secondary sources
Reliable for context and interpretation, but verify key claims against primary sources.

- Established news organizations with editorial standards (major newspapers, wire services)
- Industry reports from recognized research firms (Gartner, McKinsey, Pew, etc.)
- Expert commentary from people with verifiable credentials in the relevant field
- Organization publications where the organization has relevant expertise

**Watch for:** agenda or perspective that may shape how they present data. A trade association's research on their own industry should be used carefully.

### Tier 3 — Useful but verify
Use for leads, context, or background — not as standalone evidence.

- General reference sites (Wikipedia is a useful starting point, not an endpoint)
- Blog posts and op-eds, even from credible authors
- Social media posts, even from experts
- Content marketing or brand-published "research"
- News aggregators or publications without clear editorial standards

**How to use:** treat as a pointer to better sources, or for illustrative quotes from named individuals. Never cite Tier 3 as evidence for a factual claim without Tier 1 or Tier 2 backup.

### Tier 4 — Treat with caution
Flag explicitly if used. Often signals the user should note the limitation.

- Anonymous or unattributed content
- Sources with obvious financial or ideological stake in the answer
- Content that cannot be traced to an original source
- Outdated material in fast-moving fields

## Red flags to watch for

**Conflict of interest**: Who funded the research? Who benefits from this conclusion being true? A study on the health benefits of a product funded by the company selling that product deserves extra scrutiny — not automatic dismissal, but acknowledgment.

**Single-source claims**: If a surprising fact appears in only one place and isn't cited back to an original source, don't include it as established fact. Note it as unverified or dig for the primary source.

**Date sensitivity**: In fast-moving fields (AI, medicine, economics, tech), a source from 3 years ago may be significantly outdated. Note recency when it matters.

**Methodology problems**: For studies and surveys, ask: how large was the sample? Who was surveyed? Was the methodology described? A survey of 200 self-selected respondents is very different from a nationally representative study of 10,000.

**Circular sourcing**: Source A cites Source B which cites Source A. Both may trace back to the same original claim — or no original claim at all. Trace claims to their origin.

## How to represent source quality in output

Be transparent with the user:
- "According to a 2024 study published in [journal]..." (Tier 1)
- "Industry analysts at [firm] estimate..." (Tier 2)
- "This is widely reported but the primary data is harder to pin down..." (flagging uncertainty)
- "This claim appears frequently online but I couldn't verify it against a primary source — treat it as unconfirmed." (honest about limitation)

The user is better served by accurate uncertainty than false confidence.
