# Synthesis Framework

## What synthesis is

Synthesis is the step between "I have a lot of sources" and "here's what they mean." It's not summarizing each source in turn — that's just a list. Synthesis is finding the pattern across sources, resolving contradictions, and producing a single coherent view that's more useful than any individual source.

The goal: the user should be able to read your synthesis and understand the topic better than they would from reading the sources themselves.

## The synthesis process

### Step 1: Group by theme, not by source

Most researchers make the mistake of writing source-by-source: "Source A says X. Source B says Y. Source C says Z." This is not synthesis — it's a bibliography with commentary.

Instead, identify the 3–5 main themes or questions across all your sources, and organize the output around those. Each theme draws from multiple sources.

*Before synthesis:* "The McKinsey report says productivity increased by 13%. A Stanford study found a 5% gain. A Harvard Business Review article noted mixed results..."

*After synthesis:* "Evidence on remote work and productivity points in the same general direction — most controlled studies show moderate gains of 5–15% for individual contributors — but the picture is more mixed for collaborative or creative roles, where the data is thinner and more contested."

### Step 2: Resolve contradictions explicitly

When sources disagree, don't ignore it or pick a side without explanation. Ask why they disagree:
- Different time periods? (The answer may have changed)
- Different populations or contexts? (The answer may vary by situation)
- Different methodologies? (One may be more rigorous)
- Different definitions of the key term? (They may be measuring different things)

Name the disagreement and explain the most likely reason for it.

### Step 3: Weight by quality

Not all sources deserve equal treatment. A finding supported by three peer-reviewed studies should be stated with more confidence than one supported by a single blog post. Reflect this in your language:

- "Well-established: multiple independent studies confirm..." 
- "Supported but not conclusive: two studies suggest, though both have limitations..."
- "Contested: researchers disagree on this, with roughly equal evidence on each side..."
- "Anecdotally reported but not systematically studied..."

### Step 4: State what the research doesn't cover

Good synthesis includes honest gaps. What questions did you not find good answers to? What would the user need to research further? What caveats should they keep in mind when acting on this?

This is often the section users find most valuable — it tells them what they still don't know.

## Synthesis patterns by output type

**For decisions**: Organize around the decision criteria. What does the research say about each factor the user cares about? End with a clear summary of what the evidence points toward (if it does).

**For understanding a topic**: Organize from foundational to complex. Start with what's well-established, move to nuance, end with open questions.

**For comparing options**: Structure as a matrix — criteria on one axis, options on the other. Each cell gets a brief, honest assessment based on what the research shows. Avoid a false tie if one option is clearly better on the evidence.

**For fact-checking**: Lead with the verdict. Then present the evidence. Then note the quality of that evidence.

## Common synthesis mistakes

**The list masquerading as synthesis**: Bullet points of what each source said. No integration, no pattern, no conclusion.

**Premature resolution**: Forcing a clear answer when the evidence is genuinely mixed. Uncertainty is an honest finding — present it as such.

**Anchoring on the first source**: The first source you find shapes how you read everything after it. Actively look for sources that challenge the first one.

**Losing the user's question**: After reading 10 sources, it's easy to drift into writing about what the sources cover rather than what the user asked. Return to the original question before writing the output.

**Over-hedging**: Qualifying every sentence with "however" and "it depends" until the output is useless. Some things are clear. Say so.
