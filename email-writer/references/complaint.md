# Complaint Email Framework

## What makes complaint emails work

A complaint email has one job: get the outcome you want. This means the email must be firm, clear, and specific — but not emotional, aggressive, or threatening (at least initially). The counterintuitive truth is that professional, measured complaint emails get better results than angry ones, because they're easier for the recipient to act on and harder to dismiss.

The goal isn't to vent. It's to get resolution.

## Structure

**Subject line**
Be specific and factual, not inflammatory:
- "[Product/Service] issue — Order #12345"
- "Unresolved billing error — Account [number]"
- "Request for resolution — [service] on [date]"

Avoid: "Terrible experience", "Disgraceful service" — these put the reader on the defensive before they've read a word.

**Opening: State the problem in one sentence**
Factual, specific, no emotional language yet. What happened, when, in what context.

*"On [date], I purchased [product/service] and encountered [specific problem]."*

**Chronology: What happened**
A brief, factual account of events in order. Include:
- Dates and times where relevant
- Names of people you spoke to (if applicable)
- Order numbers, reference numbers, ticket IDs — anything that anchors the complaint in fact
- Any previous attempts to resolve this (shows good faith and escalation history)

Keep this section objective. Even if the situation was infuriating, the facts speak louder than adjectives.

**Impact: Why it matters**
One or two sentences on how this affected you. This isn't venting — it's giving the reader context for why resolution matters and what a reasonable resolution looks like.

*"As a result, I was unable to [do X] and had to [inconvenience/cost]."*

**The ask: What you want**
This is the most important part. Be specific:
- A full refund of $X
- Replacement of the product
- An explanation of what went wrong
- Compensation for consequential costs
- A written apology

Do NOT leave this vague ("I expect better" or "I want this sorted out"). Vague asks lead to partial, unsatisfying resolutions.

**Deadline**
Give a reasonable but firm deadline for response:
*"I'd appreciate a response by [date]."*

**Escalation path (optional but effective)**
Mention what you'll do next if this isn't resolved, calmly:
*"If I don't hear back by [date], I'll escalate this to [consumer protection body / credit card company / their supervisor / relevant regulator]."*

This isn't a threat — it's information. It also signals that you know what your options are.

**Close**
Polite but not warm. You're not happy. Don't pretend to be.
*"I look forward to your response."* Not "Thanks so much!" or "Best wishes."

## Tone calibration

| Situation | Appropriate tone |
|-----------|-----------------|
| First complaint to a business | Professional, measured, factual |
| After previous failed resolution | Firmer, reference prior attempts explicitly |
| Complaint to a colleague or manager | Collaborative framing, focus on impact not blame |
| Formal legal / regulatory complaint | Formal, dispassionate, precise |
| Complaint about a person's behavior | Focus on behavior and impact, not character |

## Calibrating the ask

A good resolution ask is proportionate to the harm and realistic to what the company can actually provide. Asking for too much undermines your credibility; asking for too little leaves value on the table.

- Minor inconvenience → apology + token compensation (discount, credit)
- Clear product/service failure → replacement or refund
- Significant time/money lost → full refund + compensation for costs
- Pattern of behavior / serious harm → formal apology, policy change, possible legal action

## Common mistakes

- **Too emotional**: Anger makes you easier to dismiss. Facts are harder to argue with.
- **No specific ask**: "I'm very disappointed" is not an ask. Be explicit.
- **No deadline**: Without one, resolution drifts indefinitely.
- **Exaggerating**: "This is the worst experience I've ever had" weakens credibility. Stick to facts.
- **CC'ing everyone immediately**: Escalate progressively. Start with the right contact, escalate if unresolved.
- **Apologizing**: Don't apologize for complaining. You're the wronged party.

## When the business responds poorly

If the first response is dismissive or inadequate:
- Acknowledge their response briefly
- Restate your specific ask
- Reference what the previous response failed to address
- Escalate explicitly: "I'll now be contacting [regulator / consumer protection service / their head office]"
