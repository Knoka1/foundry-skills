# Re-engagement / Win-back Email Framework

## What makes re-engagement emails work

A re-engagement email targets subscribers or customers who have gone cold — they stopped opening, stopped clicking, stopped buying. The instinct is to offer a discount or plead for attention. Neither works well. What works is being honest, being interesting, and making it easy to either re-engage or leave cleanly.

The core principle: **treat the reader as someone worth winning back, not a metric to rescue.** A re-engagement email that feels desperate or manipulative will accelerate the unsubscribe, not prevent it.

## Who this is for

There are two distinct re-engagement scenarios with different approaches:

**Lapsed subscribers** — signed up, received emails, stopped engaging (no opens/clicks for 60–180 days depending on your cadence)

**Lapsed customers** — bought once or regularly, then stopped purchasing (varies by product category — 90 days for consumables, 12+ months for software)

## Structure

**Subject line**
This is where most re-engagement emails succeed or fail. Options that work:

*Honest and direct:*
- "Are we still good?"
- "Have we lost you?"
- "It's been a while"

*Curiosity / value:*
- "Something new you might have missed"
- "We've changed — here's what's different"

*Permission-based (high performer):*
- "Should we keep sending you emails?"
- "Stay or go — your call"

Avoid: "We miss you!" (overused, feels hollow), excessive emoji, fake urgency

**Opening**
Acknowledge the gap directly. Don't pretend everything is normal. One sentence.

*"We noticed you haven't opened an email from us in a while — and we get it, inboxes are brutal."*

Or for lapsed customers:
*"It's been [X months] since your last order, and we've been busy in the meantime."*

**The reason to come back**
This is the heart of the email. Give them a genuine reason — not "we miss you" but something specific and valuable:

- What's new since they last engaged (new products, new content, improved service)
- A piece of your best content they might have missed
- A specific offer (if appropriate — see tone notes)
- A honest statement of what they'll get if they stay

**The ask**
Clear and low-friction. Options:

*For subscribers:*
- "Click here if you'd like to keep receiving [content type]"
- "Stay subscribed / Unsubscribe" — giving the out is counterintuitive but effective

*For customers:*
- "Here's [10% off / free shipping / gift] on your next order"
- "See what's new" with a direct link

**The permission reset (optional but powerful)**
Explicitly offering the unsubscribe path in re-engagement emails sounds backwards, but it works. Readers who don't want to be there will leave. Those who stay are genuinely re-engaged. Your list gets cleaner and your metrics improve.

*"If we're not for you anymore, no hard feelings — unsubscribe here and we'll stop the emails."*

## Tone calibration

| Situation | Tone |
|-----------|------|
| Newsletter / content brand | Honest, slightly self-aware, low-pressure |
| E-commerce / product | Warm but offer-forward |
| SaaS / subscription | Data-driven acknowledgment, helpful, feature-focused |
| High-end / luxury | Understated, no desperation, focus on exclusivity |

## Offer strategy

Discounts work for product re-engagement but use them carefully:

- A discount trains lapsed customers to wait for deals — use sparingly
- A "welcome back" gift or bonus feels less transactional than a percentage off
- For subscribers (not customers), a discount makes no sense — the reason to re-engage should be content value, not price

## Timing and cadence for lapsed subscribers

A 2–3 email re-engagement sequence is standard:

1. **Email 1**: Soft check-in — "still interested?" tone, your best recent content, no pressure
2. **Email 2 (1 week later)**: More direct — "we're going to remove inactive subscribers soon, click to stay"
3. **Email 3 (1 week later)**: Final notice — "this is your last email if we don't hear from you" — then actually follow through and remove them

Following through on the removal is important. Empty threats erode credibility.

## Common mistakes

- **"We miss you!"**: Overused to the point of parody. Feels like a template, not a real message.
- **Leading with a big discount**: Trains lapsed customers that going cold pays off. Reserve discounts for later in the sequence.
- **No clear CTA**: "Come back!" is not an action. Tell them exactly what to click and what happens when they do.
- **Not cleaning the list**: Re-engagement campaigns that don't result in actual list cleaning defeat the purpose. Remove subscribers who don't re-engage.
- **Too long**: A reader who hasn't opened your emails in three months won't suddenly read a 600-word email. Keep it short and direct.
