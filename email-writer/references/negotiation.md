# Negotiation Email Framework

## What makes negotiation emails work

Email negotiation is different from in-person negotiation — you can't read body language, and every word is permanent. This means precision matters more, and you have more time to be deliberate. Use that advantage.

The core principle: **frame everything as mutual benefit, anchor deliberately, and never negotiate against yourself.**

## Before writing: know your position

Answer these before drafting:
- What is your ideal outcome?
- What is your acceptable outcome (your walk-away threshold)?
- What is *their* likely ideal outcome?
- What can you offer that costs you little but has high value to them?
- What is your BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) — what do you do if this doesn't work?

The email should reflect your understanding of their position as much as your own.

## Structure

**Subject line**
Neutral and professional. Don't reveal your hand in the subject.
- "Follow-up on [role / project / contract] discussion"
- "[Your name] — re: [topic]"
- "Proposal for [engagement / terms / next steps]"

**Opening**
Brief acknowledgment of the current state. Warm but not effusive.
*"Thank you for sending over the offer / for the conversation on [date]."*

**The anchor**
State your position clearly and first. In negotiation, whoever names a number first sets the anchor — the reference point all subsequent numbers are judged against. If you're asking for more, name your number first and make it higher than your target.

Frame it with rationale, not just assertion:
- Market data ("Based on current market rates for this role/level...")
- Your value ("Given my [specific experience / track record / deliverable]...")
- Prior precedent ("In my previous role / engagement, my rate was...")

**The bridge**
Connect your position to their goals. Show you understand what they want and that your ask is compatible with it:
*"I want to make this work, and I'm confident [outcome] is achievable. I'd like to find terms that reflect that."*

**The ask**
Specific. One thing at a time. If you're negotiating multiple elements (salary + equity + start date), prioritize. Don't present a laundry list — it signals desperation and lets them give you the cheap concessions.

**Flexibility signal (optional but powerful)**
Showing controlled flexibility makes you look confident, not desperate. You're choosing to negotiate, not begging:
*"I'm open to discussing [alternative form / structure / timeline] if that's easier on your end."*

This gives them something to respond to without you having already conceded.

**Close**
Signal that you want to reach an agreement:
*"Happy to jump on a call to discuss if that's easier. Looking forward to finding something that works for both sides."*

## Tone calibration

| Situation | Tone |
|-----------|------|
| Salary negotiation | Confident, warm, data-backed |
| Freelance rate / contract | Professional, direct, business-like |
| Vendor / supplier | Collaborative, ROI-focused |
| Dispute resolution | Measured, factual, solution-oriented |
| Personal (rent, repairs, refund) | Firm but courteous |

## Specific scenario: salary negotiation

- Always negotiate. Most offers are not the final offer.
- Lead with enthusiasm for the role first — then negotiate. You want them to know you want the job.
- Name your number. "I was hoping for something closer to $X" is a complete negotiating position.
- Don't justify too much. Over-explaining weakens your anchor.
- If they say it's firm, ask about other elements: signing bonus, equity, remote flexibility, earlier review.
- Example bridge: *"I'm genuinely excited about this role and team. The offer is below what I was targeting — I was aiming for $X based on [rationale]. Is there flexibility there, or can we discuss the overall package?"*

## Specific scenario: freelance / contract negotiation

- Quote your rate without apology. Apologetic pricing undermines trust.
- If they push back, ask what budget they're working with — it reframes the conversation.
- Don't drop your rate without getting something in return (faster payment, longer contract, reduced scope).
- "If you need it at $X, I could scope it differently so it fits that budget" is better than just dropping your rate.

## Common mistakes

- **Apologizing for asking**: "I'm sorry to ask, but..." destroys your leverage before you start.
- **Giving a range**: "I'm looking for $80-90k" — they'll go to $80k. Give one number.
- **Negotiating against yourself**: Offering a concession before they've even pushed back.
- **Being vague**: "I was hoping for a bit more" — how much more? Be specific.
- **Burning goodwill**: Negotiating every single element aggressively leaves a bad taste. Pick your battles.
- **Accepting on the spot**: It's always fine to say "let me think on this and come back to you by [date]."

## When they push back

If they reject your ask:
1. Don't immediately concede
2. Ask for their reasoning: "Can you help me understand what's driving that constraint?"
3. Propose an alternative structure: smaller ask on one dimension, offset by something else
4. If truly at an impasse, make one final concession — but name it as a concession: "I'm going to come down to $X, and I want to flag that this is my final position."
