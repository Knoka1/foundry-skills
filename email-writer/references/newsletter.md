# Newsletter / Informational Email Framework

## What makes newsletters work

A newsletter's job is to be worth reading — not to sell, not to convert, not to drive clicks. The metric that matters is whether the reader feels they got something useful or interesting from the time they spent. That's what builds the long-term relationship that eventually converts.

The core principle: **give first, ask later (or never).** A newsletter that consistently delivers value earns the right to occasionally make an ask. One that leads every edition with a pitch trains readers to stop opening.

## Before writing: know what this edition is about

One clear topic or theme per edition. A newsletter that tries to cover everything covers nothing. Ask:
- What is the one thing the reader should take away from this?
- Why does this matter to them right now?
- Is this genuinely useful / interesting, or just content for content's sake?

If you can't answer those, the edition isn't ready to write.

## Structure

**Subject line**
For newsletters, the subject line builds on an established relationship — the reader already opted in, so you have more latitude than cold email. Options:

- Specific and intriguing: "Why your to-do list is lying to you"
- Direct and value-forward: "5 things I learned from 100 client calls"
- Conversational: "Something I've been thinking about"
- News-anchored: "What [recent event] means for [reader's world]"

Avoid: generic ("This week's newsletter"), misleading, or overly clickbait-y — it works once and erodes trust.

**Opening hook**
First 1-2 sentences must earn the read. Options:
- A surprising stat or fact
- A counterintuitive claim
- A short anecdote that opens a question
- A direct statement of what they're about to get and why it's worth their time

Don't open with "In this week's edition..." or "Welcome back to [Newsletter Name]!" — filler that burns goodwill.

**The body**
Depends on newsletter format — choose one and be consistent across editions:

*Essay / long-form*: One topic explored in depth. 400–800 words. Works when the writer has genuine expertise or perspective. Needs a strong POV, not just a summary of existing content.

*Roundup / curation*: 3–7 links, resources, or items with brief commentary on each. The commentary is the value — don't just list links. Tell them what to think about each one.

*How-to / framework*: Actionable content — steps, a process, a mental model. Clear structure, scannable, leaves them with something they can use immediately.

*Behind-the-scenes / personal*: Works for individual creators and small brands. What you're working on, what you learned, what surprised you. Intimacy is the product.

**The soft ask (optional)**
If there's a CTA, one only, and it should feel natural — not bolted on:
- "If this resonated, forward it to someone who'd find it useful"
- "Reply and let me know what you think — I read everything"
- A product mention that's relevant to the content, not a non-sequitur

No CTA is also fine. A purely valuable newsletter builds more long-term loyalty than one that always asks for something.

**Sign-off**
Consistent, personal, short. Establishes voice and builds familiarity over time.

## Tone calibration

| Type | Tone |
|------|------|
| B2B / professional | Authoritative but accessible, no jargon without explanation |
| Consumer / lifestyle | Warm, personal, relatable |
| Creator / individual | First-person, opinionated, honest |
| Brand newsletter | Consistent with brand voice — but warmer than marketing copy |

## Frequency and length

Match length to frequency and depth:
- Daily: Very short (100–300 words). Readers expect a quick hit.
- Weekly: Medium (300–700 words). Enough to develop one idea well.
- Monthly: Longer (500–1,200 words). More depth expected since there's more time between editions.

## Common mistakes

- **Every edition has a pitch**: Trains readers to disengage. If every newsletter ends with "buy my course", they stop reading.
- **Inconsistent voice**: Sounds like a different person each week. Voice is what readers bond with.
- **Too long, too much**: Trying to cover 8 things when one done well would have been better.
- **No point of view**: A summary of things that happened is not a newsletter. Your perspective on those things is.
- **Treating it like a press release**: Formal, corporate, passive. Newsletters work because they feel human.

## Measuring success

Open rate tells you if the subject line worked. Click rate tells you if the content connected. Reply rate tells you if it resonated deeply. Unsubscribe rate tells you if you've drifted from what people signed up for. Watch the trend, not the absolute number.
