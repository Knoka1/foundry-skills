# Follow-Up Email Framework

## What makes follow-ups work

The biggest mistake in follow-up emails is treating them as reminders. A reminder implies the other person forgot — which is mildly accusatory and puts you in a weak position. Instead, frame every follow-up as a new touchpoint that adds value or opens a new angle.

The second biggest mistake is following up too soon or too often. One unanswered email after a week is normal. It doesn't mean they're ignoring you.

## Timing guidelines

| Context | Wait before following up |
|---------|--------------------------|
| Job application | 5–7 business days |
| Sales / cold email | 3–5 business days |
| Colleague / internal | 2–3 business days |
| Urgent internal matter | 1 business day |
| After a meeting / interview | Same day or next morning |
| After a networking event | Within 24–48 hours |

## Types of follow-up

**Post-meeting / post-interview follow-up**
This is a thank-you-adjacent email that also reinforces your candidacy or positions. Send it the same day or next morning.
- Open with genuine thanks for their time
- Reference one specific thing from the conversation (shows you listened)
- Reaffirm your interest / next step
- Keep it under 150 words

**Unanswered email follow-up**
The most common scenario. Someone didn't reply to your first email.
- Don't apologize for following up (it's normal)
- Don't forward the original and say "just following up" — add something
- A new angle: a relevant piece of news, a question, a piece of value
- Give them a soft out: "Happy to hear if the timing isn't right"

**Proposal / deliverable follow-up**
You sent something and haven't heard back.
- Confirm they received it (without being passive-aggressive about it)
- Offer to answer questions or walk them through it
- Don't over-explain the document again — trust what you sent

**Action item follow-up (internal)**
Checking on a task or decision that was supposed to happen.
- Be specific: what was agreed, when it was agreed
- Be collaborative, not accusatory
- Offer to help unblock if there's a reason for the delay

## Structure

**Subject line**
Options:
- Reply to the original thread (most common — keeps context)
- "Re: [original subject]" if re-opening a cold thread
- A new hook if you're taking a different angle

**Opening**
Don't open with "I just wanted to follow up on my previous email." This is filler. Instead:
- Reference the original lightly ("Wanted to circle back on the note I sent last week")
- Lead with new value or a new hook
- Ask a simple yes/no question

**Body**
Short. Shorter than the original. The fact that you're following up means they've already seen the original context. Don't repeat it all — reference it briefly.

If adding new value: one piece of relevant information, a new question, or a fresh framing of why this matters.

**Ask**
Same rule as always: one clear, specific ask. Make it easy to answer.

**Tone**
Assume good intent — busy, not ignoring. Don't let impatience or frustration leak into the email. If you're frustrated, write a draft, wait an hour, reread it.

## Common mistakes

- **Following up too soon**: 24 hours after an email is almost always too soon.
- **"Just following up"**: Adds nothing. Makes you seem needy.
- **Guilt-tripping**: "I haven't heard back and wanted to make sure this didn't fall through the cracks" sounds passive-aggressive.
- **Too long**: Follow-ups should be shorter than the original. If it's longer, you've written too much.
- **No new value**: If the follow-up is identical to the original plus "any thoughts?", rewrite it.
- **Multiple follow-ups**: After two unanswered emails, it's time to try a different channel or accept the answer is no.

## The graceful exit

After two follow-ups with no response, one final email that gives them an easy out often works better than a third push:

*"Hi [Name] — I've reached out a couple of times without hearing back, so I'll assume the timing isn't right. I'll leave it here, but please feel free to reach out if things change. Best, [Name]"*

This works because it reduces pressure and often prompts a reply from people who felt too much friction to respond before.
