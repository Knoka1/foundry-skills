# Persuasion / Pitch Email Framework

## What makes persuasion emails work

A persuasion email asks someone to change their mind, approve something, fund something, try something, or commit to something they haven't committed to yet. This is different from a cold outreach (where you just want a reply) — here you're asking for a real decision.

The core principle: **lead with their interests, not yours.** People are persuaded by reasons that matter to them, not by your enthusiasm about your idea.

## Before writing: understand their decision-making criteria

The email should be built around what matters to *them*:
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- What risks are they worried about?
- What does success look like from their perspective?
- What objections will they have?
- Who else influences this decision?

If you don't know the answers to these questions, you're not ready to write the email yet.

## Structure

**Subject line**
Create genuine curiosity or relevance — not hype:
- "Proposal for [specific initiative]"
- "Idea for [their goal] — worth 5 minutes?"
- "[Shared context] — a thought"
- "[Their goal]: a different approach"

**Hook (1-2 sentences)**
Open with the problem, the opportunity, or the stakes — from their perspective. Not "I have an idea" but "Here's a problem worth solving."

**The problem / opportunity frame**
Make the case for *why this matters* before making the case for *your solution*. If they don't feel the problem, your solution won't land. One paragraph — specific, concrete, real.

**Your proposal**
Clear and concrete. What are you proposing, specifically? Not a vague concept — a specific action, decision, or commitment. What would "yes" actually mean?

**Why this / why now / why you**
Three questions the reader will have. Answer them:
- **Why this**: What makes this the right approach (vs. alternatives)?
- **Why now**: What's the cost of waiting? What's the opportunity being missed?
- **Why you**: What makes you the right person / team / vendor to do this?

Answer these in proportion to how skeptical the reader is likely to be.

**The ask**
One specific ask. Not a menu of options — a clear next step:
- "I'd love 30 minutes to walk you through the thinking"
- "I'm asking for $X in budget to run a 90-day pilot"
- "Can we agree to move forward with [specific decision]?"

**Social proof / risk reduction (optional but powerful)**
- What has already worked (a precedent, a test, a result)?
- What's the downside if it doesn't work? (Make the risk feel manageable)
- Who else supports this / is doing this?

## Tone calibration

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Internal pitch to leadership | Confident, concise, data-backed, ROI-framed |
| Creative pitch | Voice and vision forward, show don't just tell |
| Investor or sponsor outreach | Traction-focused, market-aware, founder conviction |
| Peer / collaborative proposal | Collaborative framing, mutual benefit |
| Grant or funding application | Formal, evidence-based, impact-focused |

## Handling objections in advance

The best persuasion emails address likely objections before they're raised. This shows you've thought it through and reduces the reader's friction.

Format: *"You might be wondering about [objection X] — here's how we've thought about that..."*

Don't pre-empt objections you can handle in a reply — only the ones that could kill the deal before you get a conversation.

## Length

This is one case where longer can be appropriate — but only if every paragraph earns its place. Trim anything that's there to make you feel thorough rather than to move the reader. A tight 300 words beats a rambling 600 words every time.

## Common mistakes

- **Leading with your credentials**: Credibility should support the argument, not precede it. Earn their interest first.
- **Vague asks**: "I'd love to discuss" is not an ask. What specifically do you want them to do?
- **Ignoring their perspective**: An email about what you want, dressed up as a proposal.
- **No urgency**: Without a reason to act now, decisions drift. Create genuine (not manufactured) urgency.
- **Too much enthusiasm, not enough evidence**: Passion is good; evidence is better. Use both.
- **Multiple asks**: Every additional ask reduces the likelihood of any one being fulfilled.

## Follow-up after a persuasion email

If you don't hear back:
- Wait 4–5 business days
- Follow up with new value or a new angle — not just a nudge
- Ask if they have concerns (sometimes surfacing the objection unblocks the decision)
- After two follow-ups, either try a different channel or accept the timing isn't right
