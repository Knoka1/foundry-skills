# Promotional / Offer Email Framework

## What makes promotional emails work

A promotional email has one job: get the reader to act on an offer. The offer is front and center — there's no pretense of relationship-building or information-sharing. The reader knows what this is, so don't bury the lead. What separates a promotional email that converts from one that gets deleted is how well you lead with benefit, create urgency, and make the action frictionless.

The core principle: **lead with what they get, not what you're selling.**

## Before writing: know your offer cold

Answer these before drafting:
- What is the offer exactly? (discount, bundle, limited product, free trial, event)
- Who is the audience? (existing customers, cold list, warm subscribers)
- What's the deadline or scarcity element? (ends Sunday, 50 left, first 100 only)
- What's the one action you want them to take?
- What objection will they have, and can you address it in one line?

## Structure

**Subject line**
The subject line must communicate the offer or its benefit — not tease it. People scan inboxes for value. If the offer is good, say it directly.

Options that work:
- Lead with the number: "40% off everything — today only"
- Lead with the benefit: "Your summer wardrobe, half price"
- Lead with scarcity: "Only 12 left at this price"
- Personalize if you have the data: "[Name], your exclusive offer inside"

Avoid: "We have something exciting to share", "Don't miss out" (vague), excessive emoji

**Opening line**
Restate or expand the offer immediately. Don't make them work for it.
*"For the next 48 hours, every product in our store is 30% off — no code needed."*

**The benefit block**
2-3 sentences on what they get and why it matters to them. Not features — outcomes. Not "our new formula" but "the same result in half the time."

If it's a product: what problem does it solve, what does it feel like to use it, who is it for.
If it's a discount: what can they get for less, what's the savings in real terms.

**Social proof (optional but powerful)**
One line — a stat, a review snippet, a recognizable name. Especially effective for new products or first-time offers.
*"4.8 stars across 2,000+ reviews."* or *"Our best-seller, back by popular demand."*

**The urgency element**
Name the deadline or scarcity clearly. Vague urgency ("act fast!") doesn't work. Specific urgency does.
*"Offer ends Sunday at midnight."* or *"Once these 50 units are gone, they're gone."*

**CTA**
One button or link. Action-oriented label — not "Click here" but "Shop the sale", "Claim your discount", "Get 40% off now". Make it impossible to miss visually.

**Footer**
Unsubscribe link (required). Keep it clean — don't clutter with secondary offers.

## Tone calibration

| Audience | Tone |
|----------|------|
| Existing loyal customers | Warm, appreciative, insider feel |
| Warm subscribers (haven't bought) | Persuasive, benefit-forward, remove friction |
| Cold / acquired list | More credibility-building, lighter on urgency |
| Premium / luxury brand | Restrained, aspirational — never pushy or discount-heavy |
| Mass market / value brand | Direct, energetic, price-forward |

## Length

Short. The offer is the message. If you need more than 150 words to explain it, simplify the offer or split it across two emails. Long promotional emails get skimmed to the CTA anyway — write for that reality.

## Common mistakes

- **Burying the offer**: Three paragraphs of brand story before mentioning the discount. Lead with the value.
- **Multiple CTAs**: "Shop now / Learn more / Follow us / Share with a friend" — pick one.
- **Fake urgency**: "Hurry, offer ends soon!" with no actual deadline. Readers have seen this and it kills trust.
- **No mobile optimization in mind**: Most promotional emails are opened on phones. Short sentences, big CTA, minimal clutter.
- **Weak subject line**: The best email body won't save a subject line that doesn't earn the open.

## Follow-up

A two-email sequence is standard for time-limited offers:
1. **Launch email**: Full offer, full explanation, full CTA
2. **Reminder email** (24 hours before deadline): Shorter, urgency-forward, reference the deadline explicitly — "Last chance: 40% off ends tonight"

Don't over-sequence. Three or more emails for a single offer starts to feel like harassment.
