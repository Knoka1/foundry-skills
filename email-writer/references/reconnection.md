# Reconnection Email Framework

## What makes reconnection emails work

Reaching out after a long gap is awkward because of the implicit question hanging in the air: *why now?* A good reconnection email answers that question naturally, without being forced or transactional. The worst reconnection emails feel like they were triggered by the sender needing something — even if that's true, the email shouldn't feel that way.

The best reconnection emails feel like the sender was thinking of the recipient specifically, not just mining their contacts list.

## The core principle

**Bridge the gap, then build forward.** Acknowledge time has passed (briefly), create a bridge from your shared history to the present, and then open a door forward. Don't pretend no time has passed — that's strange. Don't over-apologize for it either — that's performative.

## Structure

**Subject line**
Warm and recognizable, not cryptic:
- "Been too long — hope you're well"
- "Thinking of you — [shared reference]"
- "Long time! Wanted to reconnect"
- "[Shared memory / context] — [Your name]"

**Opening: Acknowledge the gap naturally**
One sentence. Don't belabor it.
*"It's been way too long since we've caught up — I was thinking about you the other day and realized I should just reach out."*

Not: "I'm so sorry I've been terrible at staying in touch — life has just been so crazy busy..." (This is about you, not them.)

**The bridge: Why you were thinking of them**
The most important part. A specific, genuine reason you thought of them:
- Something you saw/read/heard that reminded you of them
- News about them (a promotion, a project, a life event) you came across
- A conversation with a mutual contact
- Something you're working on that connects to shared history
- An anniversary, milestone, or shared memory

This is what separates a genuine reconnection from a mass blast. If you can't identify a real bridge, think harder — or be honest: *"I was going through old contacts and realized I'd lost touch with some of the people I genuinely respect — you were on the short list."*

**Update on you (brief)**
One or two sentences. What's changed for you that's relevant and interesting. Not a life recap — a headline.

**Invitation forward**
A gentle, low-pressure ask:
- "Would love to catch up over coffee if you're ever in [city]"
- "Happy to hop on a call sometime if you're up for it"
- "Would love to hear what you've been up to"

Make it easy to say yes, but also easy to not respond if they're not interested — don't guilt-trip.

## Tone calibration

| Relationship | Tone |
|-------------|------|
| Old friend | Warm, casual, personal, some humor |
| Former colleague | Friendly, professional, reference shared work context |
| Former mentor | Respectful, show growth since you last spoke |
| Former client / professional contact | Professional, mutual benefit framing |
| Distant acquaintance | Lighter touch, more explicit about who you are and how you know them |

## Length

Medium-short. 100–200 words. Long enough to feel personal, short enough that it doesn't feel like a wall of obligation.

## The elephant in the room: do you need something?

If you're reconnecting because you need a favor, introduction, or advice — that's fine, but handle it carefully:

**Option 1: Be transparent (often works best with closer relationships)**
*"I'll be honest — part of why I reached out is I'm [navigating X] and thought you might have useful perspective. But I also genuinely wanted to check in regardless."*

**Option 2: Reconnect first, ask later**
Reach out, catch up (even briefly), and then follow up with the ask separately. This feels more authentic but requires more patience.

**Option 3: Don't hide it, but don't lead with it**
Lead with genuine reconnection. At the end, mention what you're working on without making it an explicit ask. Let them offer if they're inclined.

What doesn't work: fake reconnection that's obviously just an ask in disguise. People sense it immediately and it damages the relationship more than the gap did.

## Common mistakes

- **Too apologetic about the gap**: One sentence acknowledging it is enough. Three paragraphs of guilt is uncomfortable.
- **Too generic**: "Hope you're well! Wanted to check in!" — this tells them nothing about why you're reaching out.
- **Mass-reconnection feel**: If you're sending the same email to 50 people, at least change the one personal detail.
- **Immediately pivoting to an ask**: Reconnect first. Ask second — ideally in a follow-up.
- **Over-explaining where you've been**: They asked for a catch-up, not your life story.
