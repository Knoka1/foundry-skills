# Cold Outreach Email Framework

## What makes cold outreach succeed

Cold outreach fails most often for one of two reasons: it's too self-focused ("I do X, would love to connect") or it asks for too much too soon. A cold email is not a sales pitch — it's a request for a small commitment. The goal is a reply, not a deal.

The fundamental rule: **earn the right to make an ask by demonstrating you understand their world first.**

## Structure

**Subject line**
The only job of the subject line is to get the email opened. Options that work:
- A genuine curiosity hook: "Quick question about [specific thing they do/said/published]"
- Mutual connection reference: "[Name] suggested I reach out"
- Specific and concrete (not vague): "Idea for reducing [specific pain] at [Company]"
- Conversational: "Loved your piece on X" (only if you actually did)

Avoid: "Exciting opportunity", "Following up", "Partnership", "Quick intro"

**Opening (1-2 sentences)**
Lead with *them*, not you. Options:
- Reference something specific they did, wrote, or said (shows you researched them)
- Name the shared context or mutual connection
- Name the specific pain point or opportunity you've identified in their world

Never open with: "My name is X and I work at Y..." — they can see that in the signature.

**The value hook (2-3 sentences)**
Now and only now, explain what you do — but frame it around the outcome for them, not what you offer. "We help [type of company] [achieve outcome]" not "We are a platform that does X."

Include one piece of social proof if you have it (a recognizable client name, a specific result, a credential that matters to them).

**The ask (1 sentence)**
Make it small, specific, and easy to say yes to. Not "Let's hop on a call sometime" but "Do you have 20 minutes next week to explore this?" or even smaller: "Would it be useful if I sent over a one-pager?"

**Sign-off**
Keep it short. Your name, title, one link (not five).

## Full example template

```
Subject: Idea for [their company]'s [relevant area]

Hi [First name],

[Opening that shows you know something specific about them or their company — one sentence.]

[What you do, framed as the outcome it creates for someone like them — two sentences max. One piece of credibility if you have it.]

[One simple, specific ask.]

[Name]
[Title] | [Company]
[One link]
```

## Tone calibration

- **Startup / tech**: Conversational, skip formality, get to the point fast
- **Enterprise / finance / legal**: More formal, lead with credibility earlier
- **Creative / media**: Show personality, reference their work more
- **Personal (not business)**: Even more casual, shorter, no jargon

## Length

Keep it under 150 words whenever possible. If you need more, you're explaining too much. Trust the reply to carry the conversation forward.

## Common mistakes

- **Too much about you**: If the first two paragraphs are about you, rewrite them.
- **Vague value prop**: "We help companies grow" means nothing. Be specific.
- **Multiple asks**: Pick one. More than one dilutes all of them.
- **Desperate tone**: "I'd really love the chance to..." signals low confidence.
- **No easy out**: Giving people a low-pressure way to decline (e.g., "happy to hear it's not the right time") paradoxically increases response rates.
- **No personalization**: Template-looking emails get deleted. Show you did 5 minutes of research.

## Follow-up on cold outreach

One follow-up is appropriate, 3-5 days later. Keep it shorter than the original, assume good intent (busy, not ignoring), add a small new piece of value or angle, and give a soft out: "No worries if the timing isn't right."
