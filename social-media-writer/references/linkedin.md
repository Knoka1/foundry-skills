# LinkedIn Writing Framework

## What works on LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a professional network where people share ideas, lessons, career updates, and industry perspective. The feed rewards content that is specific, personal, and genuinely useful — not polished corporate announcements or vague inspirational quotes. The algorithm favors posts that generate comments, so content that invites a response (a question, a debate, a relatable experience) tends to outperform content that just informs.

The reader on LinkedIn is typically a professional scrolling between meetings. They'll give you two seconds. If the first line earns it, they'll expand the post. If not, they scroll.

## Post formats

**The personal story + lesson**
The highest-performing format on LinkedIn. A specific story from your own experience that leads to a transferable insight. The story is the evidence; the lesson is the payoff.

Structure:
- Hook: the moment or decision, stated specifically
- Story: what happened, told briefly and honestly
- Turn: where it got interesting or went wrong
- Lesson: what you took from it that applies to others
- Invitation: a question or observation that invites the reader to engage

Length: 150–300 words. Long enough to tell the story, short enough to hold attention.

**The counterintuitive take**
State a belief that challenges conventional wisdom in your field. Back it with your experience or evidence. This performs well because people either agree (and feel validated) or disagree (and want to say so). Both generate engagement.

Structure:
- Bold claim in the hook
- Why the conventional view exists (steelman it briefly)
- Why you think it's wrong or incomplete
- What you've seen instead
- Optional: invitation for pushback

**The list / framework**
Works best when the items are specific and non-obvious. Generic lists ("5 ways to be productive") perform poorly. Specific, hard-won lists ("5 things I stopped doing after managing 20 people") perform well.

Structure:
- Hook that explains why this list matters
- Items with enough context to be useful — not just bullets with one word
- Brief closing reflection or call-to-engage

Length: 200–400 words. Use line breaks between items for scannability.

**The short observation**
A single idea, expressed in 3–5 sentences. No story, no list — just a clear, well-stated thought. Requires strong writing. Works best for high-credibility accounts but can work for anyone if the idea is genuinely sharp.

## Formatting norms

**Line breaks**: LinkedIn compresses text into a "See more" fold after about 3 lines. The first 2–3 lines must stand alone as a hook that makes the reader click "See more." Use short paragraphs (1–3 sentences) and line breaks to create breathing room in the body.

**Hashtags**: 3–5 hashtags at the end, not embedded in the text. Embedded hashtags read as noise. More than 5 hashtags signals spam to both the algorithm and the reader.

**Emoji**: Optional. Used well, they add personality. Used poorly, they look like a template. Avoid using them as bullet points — it's one of the most recognizable AI writing patterns.

**Tagging people**: Only tag someone if they're genuinely relevant (they're in the story, they'd want to know about the post). Tagging for reach is transparent and annoying.

**Images**: A relevant image or document carousel increases reach. A stock photo adds nothing.

## Hook patterns that work on LinkedIn

- **The specific number**: "I've hired 40 people. Here's the one thing that predicts who stays."
- **The admission**: "I made a mistake that cost our team 3 months of work."
- **The counterintuitive claim**: "The best managers I know rarely give feedback."
- **The bold statement**: "Most career advice is written for people who don't need it."
- **The question**: "When did you last tell someone on your team they were doing a great job? Not in a review. Just... directly."

Avoid: "I'm excited to share...", "Thrilled to announce...", "Hot take:", "Unpopular opinion:" (these have become clichés).

## Tone calibration

| Creator type | Tone |
|-------------|------|
| Executive / leader | Thoughtful, specific, earned authority — not self-promotional |
| Individual contributor | Honest, relatable, specific about their experience |
| Entrepreneur / founder | Direct, opinionated, willing to be wrong in public |
| Recruiter / HR | Warm, people-focused, avoid jargon |
| B2B brand | Useful information first, brand voice second — never the reverse |

## Common mistakes

- **Opening with "I"**: LinkedIn's algorithm and reader attention both respond poorly to posts that start with "I". Restructure to lead with the idea.
- **The humblebrag**: "Honored and humbled to announce..." — readers see through this. State the news directly.
- **Vague inspiration**: "Success is a journey, not a destination." No one learns anything. No one engages.
- **Too long**: Posts over 400 words lose most readers. Make the point faster.
- **No engagement hook**: A post with no question, no invitation, nothing to respond to will generate reads but no comments. Comments are what drive reach.
