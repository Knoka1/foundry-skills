# TikTok Writing Framework

## What TikTok writing actually means

On TikTok, "writing" primarily means two things: the spoken script (what you say on camera) and the on-screen text that reinforces or extends the video. Captions play a smaller role than on Instagram — the video is the content. But the hook (the first 1–3 seconds), the script structure, and the on-screen text all require deliberate writing to perform well.

This framework covers caption writing, hook writing, and script structure for TikTok videos.

## The hook: first 1–3 seconds

TikTok's algorithm watches whether viewers watch past the first 3 seconds. If they don't, the video gets buried. Everything about a TikTok video should serve the hook — including the on-screen text that appears in the first frame.

**What makes a TikTok hook work:**
- It creates a question the viewer needs answered ("Wait, that's actually possible?")
- It promises a specific payoff ("I made $4,000 in a week doing this")
- It challenges a belief ("Everything you know about [topic] is wrong")
- It creates pattern interrupt — something unexpected that stops the scroll

**On-screen text hooks** (appear as text overlay at the start):
- "POV: you just discovered [thing]"
- "Things nobody talks about in [industry/topic]:"
- "I tried [thing] for 30 days. Here's what happened:"
- "Story time: the time I [specific embarrassing/impressive/unusual thing]"
- "The [topic] mistake that cost me [specific consequence]"

**Spoken hooks** (first words out of your mouth):
- Start mid-sentence or mid-action — no intro, no "hey guys", no "so basically"
- "Here's what actually happens when you..."
- "Nobody tells you this, but..."
- "Stop doing [thing]. Do this instead."

## Script structure

TikTok scripts follow a tighter version of the problem-solution-CTA arc:

**Setup (3–10 seconds)**: The hook. Create the question or promise the payoff. Don't explain — just open the loop.

**Tension (10–30 seconds)**: Develop the setup. Add specificity. Make it relatable or surprising. This is where you earn the watch-through.

**Payoff (20–45 seconds)**: Deliver what you promised. The tip, the reveal, the punchline, the lesson. Be specific. Vague payoffs cause viewers to feel tricked — and they'll say so in comments.

**CTA (final 3–5 seconds)**: One action: follow for more, comment with your answer, share with someone who needs this. Don't list three CTAs.

**Total length**: 30–60 seconds is the sweet spot for most educational or storytelling content. Shorter for humor or simple tips. Longer (2–3 minutes) only when the topic genuinely requires depth and you've built audience trust.

## Caption writing

TikTok captions are limited to 2,200 characters but most viewers read only the first line before tapping to expand. The caption's job is:

1. **SEO**: TikTok's search is growing. Include 1–2 natural keywords in the caption for discoverability.
2. **Context**: Add something the video doesn't say — a resource, a link (when available), or additional detail.
3. **Engagement**: A question at the end of the caption invites comments, which boost the algorithm.

**Caption format:**
- Line 1: The key message or a question (what shows before "more")
- Lines 2–4: Additional context, steps, or detail
- Hashtags: 3–6, a mix of broad and niche. Don't use more than 8.

**Hashtags that work across most niches**: #learnontiktok, #fyp (debated effectiveness), plus 2–4 topic-specific tags.

## On-screen text

Text overlays reinforce the spoken content and make the video accessible to people watching without sound (a significant portion of viewers). Key rules:

- Match timing: text appears when the point is being made, not before or after
- Keep it short: 5–8 words maximum per text overlay
- Don't just transcribe: on-screen text should emphasize or add, not duplicate
- Readable fonts and contrast: white text with dark shadow or solid background

## Tone calibration

| Content type | Tone |
|-------------|------|
| Educational / tips | Direct, confident, no hedging — speak like you know the answer |
| Personal story | Conversational, honest, specific — talk to the camera like a friend |
| Product review | Genuine, specific about what you liked and didn't |
| Comedy / entertainment | Lean into the bit — commit fully, don't explain the joke |
| Business / brand | More human than other platforms — brands that talk like brands get ignored |

## Common mistakes

- **Long intros**: "Hey everyone, welcome back to my channel, today we're going to talk about..." — by the time you finish this sentence, 70% of your viewers have left.
- **Vague hooks**: "I have something to tell you" — about what? Give them a reason to stay.
- **Too many CTAs**: "Like, comment, share, follow, and check the link in my bio" — pick one.
- **Captions as an afterthought**: A well-written caption with keywords improves discoverability meaningfully. Don't skip it.
- **Recreating other platforms' content**: A LinkedIn post read aloud is not a TikTok. The platform has its own rhythm and expects you to match it.
