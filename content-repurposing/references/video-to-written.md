# Video / Podcast to Written Content Framework

## What this conversion requires

Video and audio content has a natural advantage: it's easy to consume. You press play and let it wash over you. Written content has a different advantage: it's scannable, searchable, and re-readable. Converting one to the other isn't about transcription — it's about reimagining the ideas for a format where the reader controls the pace.

A transcript dropped into a blog post is one of the most painful reading experiences possible. Spoken language has fillers, repetition, tangents, and a rhythm that doesn't transfer to text. The written version needs to be rewritten, not transcribed.

## The input types

**Full video/podcast transcript**: The most complete input. Contains everything but requires the most editing — filler words, false starts, repeated points, tangential stories all need to be stripped or condensed.

**Video/podcast notes or outline**: Partial input. Captures the structure and key points without the spoken transcript. Often easier to work from than a raw transcript.

**Summary of what was discussed**: The least structured input. Treat it like a research brief — organize and expand into the written format.

**Timestamps and chapter markers**: Useful for structuring longer videos. Each chapter becomes a section.

## Conversion types

### Video/Podcast → Newsletter or Email

**The goal**: A single, valuable idea delivered in a conversational tone. The newsletter is not a summary of the episode — it's the best single idea from it, developed for readers who may not have listened.

**Structure:**
- Opening hook: The sharpest idea or most surprising thing from the video/podcast
- Body: The idea developed — context, evidence, why it matters
- Practical takeaway: What the reader can do with this
- Link to the original: Optional, at the end — "If you want the full story, the episode is here."

**Length**: 200–500 words. Newsletters are read on mobile, often between tasks. Shorter is better unless your audience has opted into depth.

**What to cut**: The host's intro and outro, sponsor reads, tangents, repeated points, filler stories that don't serve the core idea.

### Video/Podcast → Blog Post or Article

**The goal**: A structured, complete piece of written content that stands alone — not a show notes page.

**Structure:**
- Headline: Rewritten for search and clarity (not the episode title)
- Intro: Hook the reader in 2–3 sentences. Different from how the video started.
- Body: Organized into clear sections with headers. Each section covers one point.
- Conclusion: A specific takeaway or call to action
- Optional: Embed the video/audio at the top or bottom

**Length**: 600–1,500 words depending on depth of original content.

**Key difference from transcription**: The blog post uses the video's ideas but is rewritten as an essay. Sentences are tighter. Repetition is cut. The structure follows logic, not the order things were said in.

### Video/Podcast → Social Posts

See `references/article-to-social.md` for the full framework — the same logic applies. Extract the best angles, write each as a native social post, don't summarize.

## Working from a transcript: the editing process

1. **First pass — cut the noise**: Remove filler words ("um", "you know", "basically", "kind of"), repeated points, and sponsor reads. A 60-minute podcast transcript may go from 10,000 words to 6,000 after this step.

2. **Second pass — identify the structure**: What are the 3–5 main ideas? Group related content together, even if it was scattered throughout the episode.

3. **Third pass — rewrite for reading**: Each section gets a header. Spoken sentences get shortened and tightened. Stories get condensed to their essential beats. Direct quotes from guests or hosts can be preserved if they're sharp.

4. **Final pass — hook and conclusion**: Write the intro from scratch (the podcast's opening rarely works as a written hook). Write a conclusion that gives the reader something to take away.

## Common mistakes

**Publishing a transcript**: Raw transcripts are almost unreadable. They're useful as a working document, not as a final product.

**Writing show notes instead of content**: "In this episode, we discuss X, Y, and Z with guest A." That's a table of contents, not content. Give readers something they can use without listening.

**Losing the voice**: Good conversion preserves the creator's voice — the specific phrases, the perspective, the personality. Don't sand it down to generic professional prose.

**One-to-one mapping**: The video's structure is optimized for watching. The written structure should be optimized for reading. These are often different.
