# Long-Form to Short-Form Framework

## What this conversion requires

Turning long content into short content is not summarizing — it's extracting. A summary captures everything at a reduced scale. An extraction finds the one thing worth isolating and presents it fully. The best short-form content from a long-form piece often doesn't feel like an excerpt — it feels like an original piece that happens to come from a larger source.

This framework covers: long video → short clip, long article → short post, long report → one-paragraph insight, long email → key message.

## The extraction mindset

Before cutting, ask: what's the single most valuable thing in this piece?

Not the most important thing to the creator — the most valuable thing to the target audience. These are sometimes the same and sometimes very different. A 45-minute podcast episode on entrepreneurship might have 43 minutes of setup and 2 minutes of a counterintuitive observation about pricing that's genuinely novel. The short-form version is those 2 minutes, framed so they stand alone.

**The five extraction targets:**

1. **The quotable moment**: A sentence or phrase that's memorable, provocative, or surprisingly clear.
2. **The peak insight**: The point where the piece delivers its highest-value idea.
3. **The story**: An anecdote or example that illustrates the larger point — often the most shareable element.
4. **The counterintuitive claim**: The moment where the piece challenges what most people assume.
5. **The practical step**: A specific action the audience can take — often buried in longer content.

## Video: Long → Short clip

**What makes a good clip:**
- It opens with something compelling — not "so, as I was saying..."
- It delivers a complete idea — the viewer understands the point without needing the full video
- It ends at a natural stopping point — not mid-sentence or mid-thought
- It's 60–180 seconds for most platforms (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts)

**Editing approach:**
- Find the moment where the speaker makes their sharpest point
- Cut in before the point is made (creates anticipation), not during or after
- Cut out after the point lands cleanly
- Add captions — most short-form video is watched without sound
- Add a hook text overlay at the start: what the clip is about in 5–8 words

**Common clip formats:**
- The hot take: a bold opinion stated clearly
- The one tip: a single specific piece of advice
- The story: a brief anecdote with a clear ending
- The revelation: a surprising fact or reframe

## Article / Report: Long → Short post or email

**Compression ratios:**

| Source length | Short-form target | Compression |
|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| 2,000-word article | 150-word LinkedIn post | ~13x |
| 10-page report | 300-word email summary | ~15x |
| 5,000-word essay | Twitter thread (10 tweets) | ~20x |

**The compression process:**

1. Identify the one-sentence core claim of the long piece
2. Find the best 1–2 pieces of evidence or examples that support it
3. Write the short version starting from the core claim — not from the beginning of the original piece
4. Add a hook at the start that earns the read
5. Cut everything that doesn't serve the core claim

**What to preserve**: The core argument, the sharpest example, the most specific data point.

**What to cut**: Context the short-form audience doesn't need, caveats that slow the momentum, secondary arguments, repetition.

## Email: Long → Key message

Long emails lose readers. The goal is to extract the one thing the recipient actually needs to act on.

**The short version of a long email has:**
- One sentence: what this is about
- One sentence: what you need from them (or what they need to know)
- One sentence: the relevant deadline or context

If you can't write the short version, the long email probably isn't clear about what it's asking for either.

## When short-form doesn't work

Not all long content compresses cleanly. Flag these situations:
- The source piece doesn't have a clear central argument — compression reveals this
- The value is in the completeness, not any individual part (a reference document, a legal contract)
- The ideas require context that can't be established in short form without misrepresenting them

In these cases, a short teaser that drives to the full piece is better than a compression that distorts it.

## Common mistakes

**Summarizing instead of extracting**: "This article covers X, Y, and Z" is a table of contents, not short-form content. Pick one thing.

**Cutting the wrong part**: Cutting the story or example and keeping the abstract claim. Stories are what make content shareable — they're usually worth preserving over the explanatory text around them.

**Starting at the beginning**: Long content often starts with setup and context. Short-form should start at the moment of highest value. Skip the preamble.

**Forgetting the hook**: The short version still needs a hook. A compressed piece that starts with "In summary..." is not compelling.
