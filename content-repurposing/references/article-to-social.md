# Article / Blog Post to Social Media Framework

## What this conversion requires

A blog post and a social media post are fundamentally different objects. A blog post is designed to be found via search, read start to finish, and referenced later. A social post is designed to stop a scroll, deliver one idea in seconds, and prompt an immediate response. The mistake most people make is treating the blog post as a script — summarizing it paragraph by paragraph. The result is a social post that reads like an executive summary no one asked for.

Good blog-to-social conversion picks one angle from the article, rewrites it for the platform's format, and makes it feel native.

## Step 1: Mine the article for social-ready material

Before writing any post, extract the following from the source article:

**The best single insight**: What's the one thing a reader would most want to share after reading this? Often it's buried in paragraph four, not in the intro.

**The most quotable line**: Is there a sentence in the article that's sharp enough to stand alone? Copy it verbatim — sometimes the best social post is a direct quote from the article.

**The counterintuitive claim**: Does the article challenge conventional wisdom anywhere? This angle often outperforms a summary on social.

**The best example or story**: A specific anecdote, case study, or data point from the article can anchor an entire social post on its own.

**The practical takeaway**: What can someone do differently after reading this? Actionable content gets saved and shared.

## Step 2: Choose the right angle for each platform

One article can generate multiple social posts — each using a different angle.

| Extraction | Works best on |
|-----------|--------------|
| The counterintuitive claim | Twitter/X (debate-starter), LinkedIn (thought leadership) |
| The best story or example | LinkedIn (personal story format), Instagram (caption) |
| The practical steps | Twitter/X thread, LinkedIn list, Instagram carousel |
| The quotable line | Twitter/X (standalone), Instagram (quote card) |
| The data point | LinkedIn (credibility post), Twitter/X (hook) |

## Step 3: Rewrite for the platform — don't summarize

**For LinkedIn:**
Don't write "In my latest article, I explore..." — that's a link share, not a post. Write a standalone post using the article's best angle. The article link goes in the first comment or at the end of the post. Structure: hook → story or insight → lesson → engagement question.

**For Twitter/X:**
Option A — Single tweet: The sharpest line or claim from the article, stated boldly.
Option B — Thread: Take the article's main points and write each as its own tweet. The first tweet is the hook; each subsequent tweet adds one new thing. The final tweet links back to the full article.

**For Instagram:**
The hook (first line before the fold) is everything. Use a compelling moment or claim from the article. The body can summarize the key lesson in 3–4 short paragraphs. Close with a question. The link goes in bio.

## How many posts can one article generate?

A thorough 1,000-word article can typically produce:
- 2–3 LinkedIn posts (different angles over time, not all at once)
- 1 Twitter/X thread + 3–5 standalone tweets
- 1–2 Instagram captions
- 1 TikTok hook / script idea

Space them out over days or weeks. The same audience seeing the same article from five different angles in one day is noise. One good post per platform, per week, from the same source material is sustainable content without the constant creation pressure.

## Common mistakes

**The link dump**: "New article! Check it out: [link]" — this gets near-zero organic reach on every platform. Write the social post as standalone content that delivers value whether or not the reader clicks.

**The summary trap**: Starting the post with "In my article, I cover X, Y, and Z..." — nobody asked for a table of contents. Pick one thing and write about that thing.

**Using the article's intro verbatim**: Article intros are written for SEO and search intent — they don't work as social hooks. Rewrite from scratch.

**Posting all angles at once**: Four posts about the same article in the same day trains your audience to mute you.
