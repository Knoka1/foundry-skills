# YouTube Script Structure Framework

## The fundamental problem scripts must solve

A viewer starts every YouTube video with a simple question: "Is this worth my time?" The script's job is to answer yes — and keep answering yes — every 30 to 60 seconds. Viewers don't leave because a video is long. They leave because they stop believing the remaining time is worth spending.

Every structural element below exists to maintain that belief.

## Full script structure

### 1. The Hook (0–60 seconds)

The hook is the most important part of the script. Read `references/hooks.md` for the full framework. In brief:

The hook must open a loop — a question, a promise, or a tension that the viewer needs to watch to resolve. It should be clear, specific, and credible. It should not explain what the video is about in the abstract ("today we're going to talk about X") — it should show why they need to care about X right now.

Good hooks are often written last, after the creator fully understands what the video delivers.

### 2. The Setup / Intro (60–90 seconds)

After the hook, give the viewer a roadmap:
- Who this video is for (stated briefly — helps the right viewer self-select in, and the wrong viewer self-select out)
- What they'll know by the end
- A brief credibility signal if relevant (why you, why now)

Keep this tight. Every second spent on setup is a second not spent on the promised content. Most intros are too long — cut them in half and you'll likely improve retention.

**What not to do**: Channel intros ("welcome back to my channel, don't forget to like and subscribe before we get started") before delivering any value. These train viewers to skip the first 30 seconds.

### 3. The Body

Structure the body around 3–5 main points, sections, or chapters. Each section should:
- Have a clear, titled purpose (even if the title is only in the script, not spoken aloud)
- Add something new rather than restating what came before
- End with a micro-hook that transitions to the next section

**Transition technique**: End each section by foreshadowing the next. "Now you know X — but there's a problem with X that most people miss. Here's what it is." This creates forward momentum and reduces drop-off between sections.

**Section length calibration:**

| Video length | Body sections | Approximate section length |
|-------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| Under 5 min | 2–3 | 60–90 seconds each |
| 5–10 min | 3–4 | 90–120 seconds each |
| 10–20 min | 4–6 | 2–4 minutes each |
| 20+ min | 5–8 | 3–5 minutes each |

**Pattern interrupts**: Every 3–5 minutes, include something that changes the pace or visual — a new angle, a cut to B-roll, an example, a story, a direct question to the viewer. Monotony is the enemy of retention.

### 4. The Payoff

Before the outro, deliver the clearest version of the video's core value. This is the moment the hook promised. If the hook said "I'm going to show you X", this is where X is delivered most concisely and memorably.

Some creators put the payoff at the end as a reward for watching through. Others deliver it mid-video and use the outro to expand on it. Either works — the key is that there is a clear payoff, and it delivers on what was promised.

### 5. The Outro (60–90 seconds)

The outro has two jobs: wrap up cleanly and prompt one action.

**Wrap-up**: A brief summary of the key takeaway (one sentence) and an acknowledgment that the viewer made it to the end.

**The call to action**: One CTA, stated specifically. Not a list:
- "Subscribe for more" (weak — why?)
- "Subscribe if you want to [specific outcome] — I post every [cadence] on [topic]" (better)
- "Watch this next: [specific video title]" — one of the highest-converting CTAs because it keeps the viewer on your channel

**What to avoid in outros**: Long-winded thanks, apologies for the video's quality, listing every social platform with a handle, asking for likes/comments/subscribes/shares all at once.

## Script length vs. video length

The rule of thumb: 130–150 spoken words per minute of video.

| Target video length | Script word count |
|--------------------|------------------|
| 5 minutes | 650–750 words |
| 8 minutes | 1,050–1,200 words |
| 10 minutes | 1,300–1,500 words |
| 15 minutes | 1,950–2,250 words |
| 20 minutes | 2,600–3,000 words |

These are for tightly scripted videos. Conversational or off-the-cuff sections reduce word count relative to time.

## Format for the finished script

Write scripts in a format the creator can read while recording:

```
[HOOK] (~45 sec)
"Most people who try [X] quit within two weeks. I almost did too. Then I figured out what I was doing wrong — and it changed everything."

[INTRO] (~30 sec)
"In this video, I'm going to show you exactly what I changed, why it works, and how you can do the same thing starting today."

[SECTION 1: The Common Mistake] (~2 min)
"The biggest mistake people make with [X] is..."

[TRANSITION]
"Now that you know what not to do, here's what actually works..."

[SECTION 2: The Fix] (~3 min)
...
```

Use speaker notes in brackets for visual cues, B-roll prompts, or pacing notes: `[cut to screen recording]`, `[pause here]`, `[show graphic]`.
